# Cooking Issues Podcast Notes

## Equipment & Technology

### Rotary Evaporator (Rotovap)
*   **Function:** Distillation equipment allowing for low-temperature distillation to isolate pure flavors (e.g., chocolate liquor without bitterness, floral notes from habaneros, fresh herbs like cilantro).
*   **Legal Status:** Illegal for producing alcoholic beverages in the US without a distiller's license. Chefs are confined to making non-alcoholic drinks to avoid endangering liquor licenses.
*   **Cost:** Ranges from $5,000 to $18,000+ depending on the model.
*   **Tradeoffs:** High cost, space requirements, and training needed. Commercial beverages often lack the longevity or production ease of lab-distilled versions.
*   **Recommendation:** If you have $20,000 to spend, contact Bukey (the manufacturer) immediately.
*   **Models Mentioned:**
    *   **Bukey:** The "butt-kicking" high-end models. The host received a new unit from them to replace an old, beat-up eBay unit.
    *   **Old eBay Unit:** Used previously; crowded together to function.

### Vacuum Sealer
*   **Model:** **Nipack MVS 31**.
*   **Type:** Chamber vacuum sealer (professional grade).
*   **Advantages:**
    *   Extends food life significantly by preventing oxidation.
    *   Prevents "warmed-over flavor" in meat.
    *   Allows repackaging of bulk meat (e.g., Costco) into usable sizes.
    *   Preserves berries (e.g., blueberries) without ice crystals.
    *   Ideal for portioning leftovers (e.g., pasta) to reheat without drying out.
*   **Tradeoffs:** Can alter texture of delicate proteins (fish, poultry) if vacuum level is too high.
*   **Recommendation:** The host prefers professional chamber sealers over consumer units (like FoodSaver).

### Sous Vide Circulator vs. Immersion Circulator
*   **Sous Vide Supreme:**
    *   **Type:** Unstirred bath.
    *   **Pros:** Easy to use, compact, holds product well.
    *   **Cons:** Bigger than necessary for home kitchens; lacks circulation, which can be an issue for long cooks or frequent batch changes.
    *   **Price:** ~$500.
*   **Stirred Bath Circulator:**
    *   **Type:** Stirred bath.
    *   **Pros:** Superior performance, especially for shorter cook times or frequent batch changes.
    *   **Price:** Just under $800 (approx. $300 more than the Supreme).
*   **Recommendation:** The stirred bath circulator is "boatloads better" and worth the extra cost. The host has never regretted buying a circulator.

### Induction Cooktop
*   **Mechanism:** Uses oscillating magnetic electric fields to heat the pan directly, not the cooktop surface.
*   **Pros:** Most efficient cooking method; heats pan directly.
*   **Cons:** Less popular in the US due to higher electricity costs compared to gas and chef familiarity.

### Dry Ice
*   **Substance:** Solid carbon dioxide.
*   **Sourcing:** Ice supply houses or welding supply shops.
*   **Cost:** ~$2/lb. Requires buying in bulk (e.g., 20lb blocks).
*   **Storage:** Cannot be stored in a fridge (sublimates). Use a disposable styrofoam cooler; lasts hours to a day depending on insulation.
*   **Safety:**
    *   **Do NOT** seal dry ice in a soda bottle (pressure buildup can reach 80+ psi, causing explosion).
    *   Wear gloves to prevent cold burns (safer than liquid nitrogen regarding glove penetration).
    *   Wear safety glasses when chipping to prevent eye injury from flying shards.
    *   Do not use in living rooms (CO2 buildup risk).

## Techniques & Parameters

### French Fry Optimization
*   **Goal:** Crispy exterior, fluffy interior, non-greasy, good when cold.
*   **Process:**
    1.  **Blanching:** Soak in enzyme (**Pectinex SPL**) for a couple of hours to break down pectin and allow starch swelling.
    2.  **Boil:** Blanch in boiling salted water to cook and flavor.
    3.  **Fry:** Double fry (fry once, then fry again).
*   **Result:** Arguably the most delicious fries; eliminates the need for a drying step which often causes hollowness.
*   **Note:** Traditional double-fry (fry-cool-fry) is considered "kindergarten" technique compared to the enzyme method.

### Brisket Cooking
*   **Technique:** Low and slow braising.
*   **Science:** Overcook the meat to break down collagen into gelatin, which remoistens the meat.
*   **Temperature:** ~52°F (approx. 11°C) for a long time (specific time dependent on weight).
*   **Sear:** Debatable if necessary for Maillard reaction, but essential for texture development in some contexts.
*   **Common Failure:** Undercooking (insufficient time for collagen conversion).

### Sous Vide Tenderloin
*   **Challenge:** Tenderloin lacks connective tissue. Cooking too long or too hot makes it fibrous/mushy.
*   **Temperature:** Low end of the scale, around **44°C (111°F)**.
*   **Time:** Short duration, approximately **45 minutes**.
*   **Finish:** Must be seared after cooking.
*   **Safety:** Safe to eat after searing (surface kills bacteria; interior muscle is sterile).

### Dry Ice Chilling for Drinks
*   **Application:** Maintaining cold temperature and carbonation for pitchers of drinks (e.g., Gin & Tonics) over long periods (parties).
*   **Method:**
    *   Use a narrow pitcher.
    *   Add a couple of "rocks" (chunks) of dry ice.
    *   Stir after 10–15 minutes.
    *   Drink will be lightly carbonated and stay cold all night.
*   **Caveats:**
    *   Do not let chunks float freely if they can burn the tongue.
    *   Drink will become slightly carbonated (unwanted for some).
    *   Better than freezing in a standard freezer where drinks warm up on the counter.

### Vacuum Sealing Levels
*   **Red Meat:** Heavy vacuum recommended to prevent oxidation and warmed-over flavor.
*   **Fish & Poultry:**
    *   **Avoid:** Full/heavy vacuum.
    *   **Reason:** High vacuum pushes water out of the muscle fibers, resulting in a grainy, fibrous texture upon chewing (like a brick).
    *   **Recommendation:** Use the **minimum vacuum** necessary to seal the bag around the food.
    *   **Alternative:** Bag fish/poultry with oil or liquid to prevent crushing.
    *   **Test:** Compare 3 identical pieces sealed at 72%, 99%, and full vacuum to taste the difference.

## Ingredients & Functional Roles

*   **Pectinex SPL:** Natural enzyme used to break down pectin on the outside of potatoes, allowing starch to swell and form a crust without requiring a drying step.
*   **Gins:**
    *   **Hendrick's:** Pricey, good for specific applications.
    *   **Tanqueray:** High proof, easy to mix.
    *   **Bombay Sapphire:** Traditional choice.
    *   **Oxley:** Good, non-traditional taste.
    *   **Beefeater:** Popular among bartenders.
    *   **Plymouth:** "Gateway gin"; light on botanicals/juniper, good for those who dislike gin.
*   **Natto:** Japanese fermented soybeans. Host dislikes it; Nastasha hates it.

## Strong Opinions & Recommendations

*   **Molecular Gastronomy:** The term is meaningless, sounds disgusting ("gas and trochonomy"), and is a marketing gimmick pioneered by Hervé This (described as a charlatan). Chefs do not want to be labeled this. Techniques should be used to improve flavor/texture, not as gimmicks.
*   **Foams:** Making foam is a skill, but rarely necessary. Don't foam everything just because you can.
*   **Vacuum Sealers:** Consumer units (FoodSaver) are inferior to professional chamber units (Nipack).
*   **Sous Vide:** A stirred bath circulator is vastly superior to an unstirred immersion circulator (Sous Vide Supreme).
*   **Dry Ice:** Essential for keeping drinks cold and carbonated at parties; cheap investment compared to the waste of warm, flat drinks.
*   **Induction:** Best cooking method technically, but hindered by US electricity costs and chef habits.
