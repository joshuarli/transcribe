# Cooking Issues Podcast Notes

## Equipment & Technology

### Rotary Evaporator (Rotovap)
*   **Function:** Distillation equipment allowing for low-temperature distillation to isolate pure flavors (e.g., chocolate liquor without bitterness, habanero floral aroma without spice, fresh herbs).
*   **Legal Status:** Illegal for producing alcoholic beverages in the US without a distiller's license. Chefs are confined to making non-alcoholic drinks; otherwise, they risk their liquor license.
*   **Cost:** Ranges from $5,000 to $18,000+ depending on the model.
*   **Tradeoffs:** High cost and space requirements limit restaurant adoption in the US, though some exist in Chicago.
*   **Recommendation:** If you have $20,000 to spend, contact Bukey (manufacturer) immediately for a new unit. Avoid older, beat-up models.
*   **Applications:** Creating "booze" from non-alcoholic sources (chocolate, peppers, herbs) for flavor extraction.

### Vacuum Sealer
*   **Model Mentioned:** Minipack MVS 31 (chamber style).
*   **Function:** Extends food life by preventing oxidation; allows repackaging bulk meat (e.g., Costco) into usable sizes; preserves berries without ice crystals.
*   **Vacuum Levels & Recommendations:**
    *   **Red Meat:** Heavy vacuum recommended to remove oxygen and prevent warmed-over flavor.
    *   **Fish & Poultry:** Use **low vacuum** only. High vacuum crushes the meat structure, causing water to rush out during cooking, resulting in a fibrous, grainy, brick-like texture.
    *   **Technique:** Bag fish/poultry with oil or liquid to prevent crushing. Seal just enough to get a good seal around the food.
    *   **Testing:** Dave recommends testing three identical pieces (sealed at 72%, 99%, and full vacuum) to taste the difference.
*   **Leftovers:** Bagging leftovers after cooking prevents drying out during reheating.

### Sous Vide Circulator vs. Immersion Circulator
*   **Sous Vide Supreme:**
    *   **Type:** Unstirred bath.
    *   **Pros:** Easy to use, compact, holds product well.
    *   **Cons:** Larger than necessary for home kitchens; lacks circulation, which can be an issue for longer cooks or frequent batch changes.
    *   **Price:** ~$500.
*   **Stirred Bath Circulator:**
    *   **Type:** Stirred bath.
    *   **Pros:** Superior circulation; better for shorter cook times and frequent batch changes.
    *   **Price:** ~$800 (approx. $300 more than Supreme).
    *   **Dave's Opinion:** "Boatloads better" than the Supreme. Never heard anyone regret buying a circulator.
    *   **Recommendation:** Get a circulator first, then play around. Ideal for lamb chops (e.g., 41°C for 1 hour + sear).

### Induction Cooktop
*   **Mechanism:** Uses oscillating magnetic electric fields to heat the pan directly, not the air.
*   **Efficiency:** Most efficient cooking method; reduces heat in the kitchen.
*   **Adoption:** Less popular in the US than Europe due to higher electricity costs and chef familiarity with gas.

### Dry Ice (Solid CO2)
*   **Sourcing:** Ice supply houses or welding supply shops. Cost: ~$2/lb (buy in 20lb blocks).
*   **Storage:** Cannot be stored in a fridge (sublimates). Use a disposable styrofoam cooler; lasts hours to a day depending on insulation.
*   **Safety:**
    *   **Do NOT** seal dry ice in a soda bottle (pressure buildup to 80+ psi causes explosion).
    *   **Do NOT** move into a living room (house fills with CO2).
    *   **Handling:** Wear gloves (cold burns) and safety glasses (flying shards).
*   **Drink Chilling Technique:**
    *   **Goal:** Maintain carbonation and temperature for long periods (e.g., party pitchers).
    *   **Method:** Use a narrow pitcher. Add a few rocks of dry ice (do not overdo it; do not freeze the drink). Stir after 10–15 minutes.
    *   **Result:** Drink stays cold and lightly carbonated all night.
    *   **Application:** Gin and Tonics (prevents them from turning flat/warm).

## Techniques & Parameters

### French Fry Optimization
*   **Goal:** Crispy outside, fluffy inside, non-greasy, good when cold.
*   **Process:**
    1.  **Blanching:** Soak in enzyme (**Pectinex SPL**) for a couple of hours to break down pectin and allow starch swelling.
    2.  **Boil:** Blanch in boiling salted water to cook and flavor.
    3.  **Fry:** Fry 3 times (or 2 times after the enzyme step, though Dave mentions a 3-time process for optimal results).
*   **Note:** Traditional double-frying is considered "kindergarten" level compared to the enzyme method.
*   **Outcome:** No need for a drying step after blanching.

### Brisket Cooking
*   **Problem:** Tough cut with high connective tissue; requires collagen breakdown into gelatin to remoisten meat.
*   **Method:** Low and slow (braise-like).
*   **Temperature:** ~52°C (125°F) for a long duration.
*   **Sear:** Sear first for Maillard reaction (Dave speculates on Texas style).
*   **Warning:** Do not treat like a standard braise if you want brisket flavor; low temp is essential.

### Sous Vide Tenderloin
*   **Challenge:** Tenderloin lacks connective tissue. Cooking too long or too hot makes it fibrous/mushy.
*   **Temperature:** Low end of the scale, specifically **54°C (130°F)**.
*   **Time:** **45 minutes**.
*   **Safety:** Safe to eat after searing (kills surface bacteria), though not technically "code" safe without sear.
*   **Finish:** Sear immediately after cooking.

### Dry Ice Drink Chilling (Caller Q&A)
*   **Caller:** Carolyn from Massachusetts.
*   **Question:** Benefits of chilling drinks over dry ice.
*   **Answer:**
    *   **Dilution Control:** Perfect dilution achieved before chilling; drink stays cold without warming up on the counter.
    *   **Carbonation:** Drink becomes slightly carbonated (good for gin and tonics).
    *   **Duration:** Stays cold and carbonated all night.
    *   **Visual:** Creates a mist effect.
    *   **Caution:** Do not let chunks float freely (burn risk); do not seal in containers.

### Gin Recommendations
*   **Hendrick's:** Pricey, favorite for specific applications.
*   **Tanqueray:** High proof, easy to mix.
*   **Bombay Sapphire:** Traditional choice (Dave's dad preferred white-labeled Bombay).
*   **Oxley:** Good, different taste profile.
*   **Beefeater:** Popular among bartenders.
*   **Plymouth:** "Gateway gin." Light on botanicals/juniper; good for those who dislike traditional gin flavors.

## Ingredients & Functional Roles

*   **Pectinex SPL:** Natural enzyme (non-GMO) used to break down pectin on the outside of French fries, enabling rapid starch swelling for crust formation.
*   **Collagen:** Connective tissue in meat (brisket) that breaks down into gelatin over long, low-heat cooking, remoistening the meat.
*   **Dry Ice (CO2):** Solid carbon dioxide. Used for rapid chilling and maintaining carbonation in beverages. Sublimes (turns to gas) rather than melting.
*   **Natto:** Japanese fermented soybeans. Dave notes Nastasha hates it; claims even Japanese people pretend to like it.

## Strong Opinions & Recommendations

*   **Molecular Gastronomy:** Dave hates the term. It is meaningless, sounds disgusting ("gas and trochonomy"), and is a marketing term pioneered by Hervé This (whom Dave calls a charlatan). All cooking manipulates molecules; the term pigeonholes chefs incorrectly.
*   **Foams:** Important skill, but rarely used unless necessary. Don't foam just to foam.
*   **Vacuum Sealing Fish:** Never use full vacuum on fish; it ruins texture. Use low vacuum and oil.
*   **Circulators:** Highly recommended over immersion circulators for performance.
*   **Dry Ice Safety:** Never seal dry ice in a bottle; always wear gloves and eye protection.
*   **Tenderloin:** Cook at 54°C for 45 minutes max to avoid fibrous texture.
