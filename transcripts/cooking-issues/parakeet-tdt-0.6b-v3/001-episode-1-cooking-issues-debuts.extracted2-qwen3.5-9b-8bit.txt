# Cooking Issues Podcast Notes

## Equipment & Technology

### Rotary Evaporator (Rotovap)
*   **Function:** Distillation equipment allowing for low-temperature distillation to isolate pure flavors (e.g., chocolate liquor, floral pepper aromas, fresh herbs) without bitterness or heat damage.
*   **Legal Status:** Illegal for producing alcoholic beverages in the US without a distiller's license; chefs are confined to non-alcoholic applications (water-based extractions).
*   **Cost:** Ranges from ~$5,000 to ~$18,000 depending on model.
*   **Tradeoffs:** High cost, space requirements, and training needed vs. superior flavor purity.
*   **Recommendation:** If budget allows (~$20k), contact Bukey (manufacturer) for a new unit. Avoid older, beat-up models.
*   **Applications:**
    *   Chocolate liquor: Extracts cocoa flavor without bitterness (bitterness does not distill).
    *   Habanero peppers: Extracts floral aroma without spice.
    *   Fresh herbs: Best method for preserving delicate flavors (e.g., cilantro).

### Vacuum Sealer
*   **Model:** Minipack MVS 31 (Chamber style).
*   **Function:** Removes air to prevent oxidation, extend shelf life, and modify meat texture.
*   **Vacuum Levels:**
    *   **Red Meat:** Heavy vacuum recommended to remove oxygen and prevent "warmed-over flavor."
    *   **Fish & Poultry:** **Low vacuum** recommended. High vacuum crushes the meat structure, causing water to rush out during cooking, resulting in a fibrous, grainy, brick-like texture.
    *   **Technique:** Bag fish/poultry with oil or liquid to prevent crushing. Seal at minimum vacuum necessary for a good seal.
*   **Applications:**
    *   Leftovers: Prevents drying out during reheating.
    *   Blueberries: Preserves without ice crystal formation.
    *   Portioning: Repackages bulk meat (e.g., Costco) into usable sizes.

### Sous Vide Circulator vs. Immersion Circulator
*   **Sous Vide Supreme:**
    *   **Type:** Unstirred bath.
    *   **Pros:** Easy to use, compact, holds product well.
    *   **Cons:** Larger than necessary for home use; lacks circulation, leading to potential temperature gradients in longer cooks or high-turnover scenarios.
    *   **Price:** ~$500.
*   **Stirred Bath Circulator:**
    *   **Type:** Stirred bath (continuous circulation).
    *   **Pros:** Superior temperature uniformity; easier to store (size of stick blender); better for short-term items and frequent batch changes.
    *   **Price:** ~$800.
    *   **Recommendation:** Strongly prefer stirred bath circulators for performance. "Never regretted buying a circulator."

### Induction Cooktop
*   **Mechanism:** Uses oscillating magnetic electric fields to heat the pan directly, not the cooktop surface.
*   **Efficiency:** Most efficient cooking method; reduces kitchen heat.
*   **Adoption:** Less popular in the US than Europe due to higher electricity costs and chef familiarity with gas.

### Dry Ice (Solid CO2)
*   **Sourcing:** Ice supply houses or welding supply shops.
*   **Cost:** ~$2/lb; typically sold in 20lb bags (~$40 investment).
*   **Storage:** Sublimates over time; cannot be stored in a fridge. Use a disposable styrofoam cooler for hours to a day.
*   **Safety:**
    *   **Do NOT** seal dry ice in a soda bottle (pressure buildup to 80+ psi causes explosion).
    *   Wear gloves to prevent cold burns (safer than liquid nitrogen regarding glove penetration).
    *   Wear safety glasses when chipping (hard pieces can fly into eyes).
    *   Do not use in living rooms (CO2 accumulation risk).
*   **Application:** Chilling pre-batched drinks (e.g., pitcher of Gin & Tonics) to maintain carbonation and cold temperature throughout an event.
*   **Technique:** Use a narrow pitcher; add chunks (not too wide); stir after 10-15 minutes. Drink will be lightly carbonated and stay cold.

## Techniques & Parameters

### French Fry Optimization
*   **Goal:** Crispy exterior, fluffy interior, non-greasy, good when cold.
*   **Process:**
    1.  **Blanching:** Soak in enzyme (**Pectinex SPL**) for a couple of hours to break down pectin and allow starch swelling.
    2.  **Cooking:** Blanch in boiling salted water (kills enzymes, flavors potato).
    3.  **Drying:** Optional/Reduced (enzyme step reduces need).
    4.  **Frying:** Double fry (initial fry to cook/kill enzymes, second fry to crisp).
*   **Result:** Arguably the most delicious fries; no hollow centers.

### Brisket Cooking
*   **Problem:** Tough cut with high connective tissue; requires collagen breakdown into gelatin to remoisten meat.
*   **Method:** Low and slow braising.
*   **Temperature:** ~52°F (approx. 11°C) for a long duration.
*   **Note:** Must cook long enough for collagen conversion. Searing first is debated (Maillard reaction importance), but low temp is critical.
*   **Alternative:** Braising tastes like braise, not brisket.

### Sous Vide Tenderloin
*   **Challenge:** Tenderloin lacks connective tissue; overcooking (even at low temps) leads to fibrous/mushy texture.
*   **Temperature:** 54°C (129°F).
*   **Time:** 45 minutes.
*   **Finish:** Sear immediately after.
*   **Safety:** Safe to eat after searing (surface kills bacteria; interior muscle is sterile).

### Dry Ice Drink Chilling
*   **Benefit:** Maintains carbonation and cold temperature for hours (unlike ice which melts and warms drinks).
*   **Technique:** Add chunks to pitcher; stir after 10-15 mins.
*   **Side Effect:** Drink becomes slightly carbonated.

## Ingredients & Functional Roles

*   **Pectinex SPL:** Natural enzyme (non-GMO) used to break down pectin on the outside of French fries, facilitating starch swelling and crust formation.
*   **Gins:**
    *   **Hendrick's:** Pricey, good for specific applications.
    *   **Tanqueray:** High proof, easy to mix.
    *   **Bombay Sapphire:** Traditional preference.
    *   **Oxley:** Good, non-traditional taste.
    *   **Beefeater:** Popular among bartenders.
    *   **Plymouth:** "Gateway gin"; light on botanicals/juniper, good for those who dislike gin.
*   **Collagen:** Connective tissue in meat that breaks down into gelatin during long, low-heat cooking, providing moisture and tenderness.

## Strong Opinions & Recommendations

*   **Molecular Gastronomy:** Hate the term. It is meaningless, sounds disgusting ("gas and trochonomy"), and is a marketing term pioneered by Hervé This (described as a charlatan). All cooking manipulates molecules; the term pigeonholes chefs incorrectly.
*   **Foams:** Important skill, but rarely used unless necessary. Don't just foam weird things for the sake of "gastronomy."
*   **Vacuum Sealing:** Essential for home cooks for leftovers and portioning. Professional chamber sealers (Minipack) are superior to consumer units (FoodSaver).
*   **Circulators:** Highly recommended over immersion circulators for performance.
*   **Dry Ice:** Excellent for parties to keep drinks cold and carbonated; cheap investment compared to the waste of warm, flat drinks.
*   **Cooking Philosophy:** Techniques should be used to improve flavor or achieve a desired effect, not as gimmicks. "Marriage of technique and flavor."
