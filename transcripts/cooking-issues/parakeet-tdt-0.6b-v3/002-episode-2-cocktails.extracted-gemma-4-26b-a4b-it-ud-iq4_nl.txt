### Curing & Vacuum Sealing
* **Vacuum Curing Advantages**: 
    * Reduces brine volume required.
    * Enables dry rubs.
    * Accelerates penetration significantly (e.g., pork belly can be cured through overnight).
* **Technique**: Use a high vacuum to accelerate penetration.
* **Caveat**: For long-cured items intended for flavor development (e.g., ham), avoid accelerating the process via vacuum, as flavor is tied to time.
* **Ingredients**: 
    * **Nitrates/Nitrites**: Used in curing powders (e.g., Instacure, Morton's Tender Quick). Essential for curing; naturally occurring in salt deposits.

### Sous Vide: Chicken
* **Parameters**: 
    * **Temperature**: 63°C (approx. 145°F) for ~1 hour. 
    * **Texture adjustment**: 64°C for firmer texture. Do not exceed 65°C.
* **Technique**: 
    * **Bagging**: Use Ziploc bags rather than vacuum sealing to avoid a "canned" texture. 
    * **Sealing Ziploc**: Submerge the bag in water to allow water pressure to displace air for a tight seal.
    * **Liquid**: Use small amounts of olive or coconut oil. Avoid using highly flavorful oils as they can create strange taste interactions.
    * **Brining**: Optional; improves salt levels and texture but is not required for temperature safety.
* **Equipment**: 
    * **Immersion Circulator**: Maintains precise water temperature to prevent overcooking and drying.

### Lobster Preparation
* **Issue**: Large/old lobsters have coarse, rubbery muscle fibers.
* **Technique for Old/Large Lobster**:
    1. **Steam**: Steam for 8 minutes to kill the lobster and separate meat from the shell.
    2. **Extraction**: Use metal shears to remove meat from thick shells.
    3. **Slicing**: Slice tail/meat into thin discs. This cuts "with the grain," shortening the fibers and improving texture.
    4. **Poaching**: Ziploc bag the slices with butter and quick-poach them individually to prevent mushiness.

### Cocktail Pre-batching
* **Theory**: 
    * Shaken cocktails are difficult to pre-batch because the specific texture (aeration/emulsion) is hard to replicate without liquid nitrogen.
    * Stirred cocktails are ideal for pre-batching as stirring is primarily a chilling and diluting process.
* **Technique (Calculating Dilution via Weight)**:
    1. Prepare a single cocktail using volume (jiggers).
    2. Weigh the initial ingredients on an accurate scale (**Initial Weight**).
    3. Prepare the drink normally (add ice, stir, strain).
    4. Weigh the final, strained cocktail (**Final Weight**).
    5. **Water to add to batch** = Final Weight - Initial Weight.
* **Note**: Do not rely on "percentage" guesses (e.g., 25%); use weight for precision.

### Sous Vide: Temperature & Timing
* **General Principle**: Heating is non-linear; formulas such as "minutes per pound" or "minutes per inch" are inaccurate.
* **Empirical Timing Benchmarks**:
    * **Egg**: 45–60 minutes to reach core temperature.
    * **Chicken Torchon**: ~1 hour.
    * **36-inch Striped Bass**: ~3 hours.
* **Reference Material**: Consult Douglas Baldwin’s mathematical tables or Nathan Myhrvold’s tables (found on e-gullet) for calculating time based on food dimensions.

### Pâte de Fruit
* **Problem**: Fruit puree browning/burning at 180°F when adding sugar and pectin.
* **Likely Cause**: Pan temperature/scorching from uneven heat.
* **Recommendations**:
    * **Equipment**: Use an induction burner for precise temperature control.
    * **Pectin Requirements**: Requires acidity and high soluble solids (sugar) to form a gel.
    * **Advanced Technique**: Use a Rotovap to evaporate water at lower temperatures to avoid heat damage, though the mixture must still reach boiling temperatures to activate pectin.
