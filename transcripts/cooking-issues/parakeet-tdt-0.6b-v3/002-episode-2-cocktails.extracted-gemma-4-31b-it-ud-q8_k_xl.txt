### Meat Curing & Processing
**Vacuum Curing**
* **Technique:** Using a vacuum sealer to accelerate brine/cure penetration.
* **Advantages:** Requires less brine; allows for drier rubs; significantly faster penetration (e.g., pork belly can be cured overnight).
* **Parameters:** Dave typically uses high vacuum, though some chefs suggest extremely high vacuum may hinder penetration.
* **Recommendation:** Do not use vacuum acceleration for long-cured items (e.g., traditional hams) as the flavor develops through the duration of the curing process.
* **Ingredients:** Curing powders (nitrates/nitrites) are effective and traditional.

**Large/Old Lobster Preparation**
* **Problem:** Older lobsters have coarser muscle fibers; traditional long cooking makes them rubbery/tough.
* **Technique for very large specimens (e.g., 20lb):**
    1. **Initial Cook:** Steam for 8 minutes (kills the animal and loosens meat from shell).
    2. **Extraction:** Use metal shears to remove meat from thick shells.
    3. **Fabrication:** Slice meat into thin discs (cuts across the grain to shorten coarse fibers).
    4. **Finish:** Ziploc bag slices with butter and quick-poach individually.

**Exotic Meats**
* **Recommendation:** Use sous vide for tough, older, or game meats (e.g., lion, bear, yak).

### Sous Vide & Low-Temperature Cooking
**Skinless Chicken Breast**
* **Parameters:** 63°C for ~1 hour. 64°C for a firmer texture; do not exceed 65°C.
* **Bagging Technique:** Use Ziploc bags (water displacement method) rather than vacuum sealing. 
* **Reasoning:** Vacuum sealing creates a "canned chicken" texture that is overly juicy/leaky upon the first bite and dry on subsequent chews.
* **Brining:** Not necessary to prevent overcooking, but recommended for improved internal salt levels and texture.

**Timing & Temperature Calculations**
* **Resources:** Douglas Baldwin (Sous Vide primer) and Nathan Mirvold (tables on e-gullet).
* **Recommendation:** Avoid linear formulas (e.g., "X minutes per pound/inch") as heat penetration is non-linear. 
* **Approach:** Use mathematical tables or develop a personal database of specific items (e.g., eggs: 45–60 mins; rolled chicken: ~1 hour; 36" striped bass: 3 hours).

### Confections & Pectin
**Pâte de Fruit**
* **Process:** 
    1. Heat fruit puree and sugar to 180°F.
    2. Add pectin, sugar, and glucose syrup.
    3. Heat to 225°F.
    4. Pour onto a Silpat to set.
* **Prevention of Scorching:** Use an induction burner for superior temperature control over open flames.
* **Pectin Requirements:** To gel, pectin requires heat (near boiling), acidity, and high soluble solids (sugar) to bind remaining water.
* **Equipment Limitations:** Immersion circulators/water baths cannot be used because the recipe requires the evaporation of water to reach the necessary solids concentration.
* **Experimental Idea:** Using a rotary evaporator (rotovap) to remove water at lower temperatures.

**Mana (Ingredient)**
* **Properties:** Wild, sweet substances consisting of dried tree sap or honeydew (aphid secretions).
* **Characteristics:** Unique chewy texture; flavor varies significantly between bites.
* **Applications:** Mixed with bourbon or processed into candy with sugar and nuts.

### Cocktail Science
**Shaking vs. Stirring**
* **Shaking:** Primarily affects the **texture** of the drink. Difficult to emulate in pre-batched cocktails unless using liquid nitrogen to chill a pre-diluted mixture.
* **Stirring:** Primarily a process of **chilling and dilution** without adding texture. Ideal for pre-batching.

**Pre-Batching Dilution Method**
To determine the exact water weight needed for a pre-batched stirred cocktail:
1. Weigh the liquor and ingredients before mixing.
2. Prepare a single drink traditionally (stir with ice and strain).
3. Weigh the final chilled cocktail.
4. **Calculation:** $\text{Final Weight} - \text{Initial Ingredient Weight} = \text{Water to add to batch}$.
* **Warning:** Do not determine dilution by tasting room-temperature mixtures; the balance changes once the drink is chilled.
