# Curing & Preservation

### Vacuum Curing
*   **Technique:** Packing meat in a vacuum bag with brine or dry rub accelerates cure penetration significantly compared to traditional methods.
*   **Parameters:**
    *   **Pork Belly:** Can achieve near-total cure penetration overnight in a vacuum bag.
    *   **Vacuum Level:** High vacuum is generally used, though some professionals argue super-high vacuum may hinder penetration at extreme levels (unverified by host).
*   **Ingredients:**
    *   **Curing Powders:** Instacure or Morton's Tender Quick (Salt + Nitrates/Nitrites).
    *   **Safety Note:** Nitrates/nitrites are natural impurities in salt deposits and have been used for millennia; not "artificial crap."
*   **Recommendations:**
    *   **Do Not Accelerate Long Cures:** Avoid vacuum acceleration for long-cured items (e.g., traditional hams) where flavor development depends on time.
    *   **Equipment:** Vacuum marinator recommended for testing optimum vacuums.

# Sous Vide & Low-Temperature Cooking

### Chicken Breast (Skinless)
*   **Technique:** Immersion circulator (sous vide) for precise temperature control to prevent drying.
*   **Equipment:** Immersion circulator.
    *   **Bagging Method:** **Do not vacuum seal.** Use Ziploc bags.
    *   **Reasoning:** Vacuum sealing creates a "canned" texture; the meat becomes too juicy initially, then dries out during subsequent chews. Ziploc bags allow for a more natural texture.
    *   **Water Displacement:** Seal bag almost full, dunk in water to displace air before submerging in bath.
*   **Parameters:**
    *   **Temperature:** 63°C (145°F) for ~1 hour.
    *   **Adjustment:** 64°C (147°F) if a firmer texture is desired. Do not exceed 65°C.
    *   **Oil:** Any oil works (chicken fat, olive, coconut). Olive oil may alter taste interaction; use sparingly.
    *   **Brining:** Not strictly necessary for texture/safety in this method, but increases internal saltiness for better taste.
    *   **Best Use:** Cold preps (salads) or chopped applications; skinless breasts are naturally soft.

### Pre-batching Cocktails
*   **Technique:** Determining exact dilution for pre-batched stirred cocktails.
*   **Method:**
    1.  Make a single drink using jiggers (volume).
    2.  Weigh the drink on an accurate scale ($W_{start}$).
    3.  Add ice, stir, strain.
    4.  Weigh the final drink ($W_{final}$).
    5.  Calculate water needed: $W_{final} - W_{start}$.
    6.  Apply this ratio to the batch.
*   **Key Concepts:**
    *   **Shaking vs. Stirring:** Shaking creates texture (aeration/emulsification); stirring is purely chilling/diluting.
    *   **Pre-batch Suitability:** Stirred drinks (e.g., Manhattan) are easily pre-batched. Shaken drinks (e.g., Daiquiri) are difficult to emulate without liquid nitrogen or extreme chilling.
    *   **Dilution Timing:** Dilute before chilling; balancing at room temperature then chilling leads to off-balance drinks.

# Exotic Meats & Game

### Sourcing & Ethics
*   **Sources:** Chicago butcher shop (Czimer) sources from animal brokers.
*   **Origin:** Meat is a byproduct of animals (lions, bears, etc.) killed due to being unwanted pets or failed breeders, not slaughtered for food.
*   **Specific Meats:** Lion, Black Bear, Raccoon, Beaver (tails), Yak.
*   **Cooking Method:** Sous vide recommended for tough, older game meats to tenderize.

### Historical Context: Fast Days & Classification
*   **Beaver Tails:** Historically classified as "fish" (not meat) during Catholic fast days (Lent/Fridays), allowing consumption when meat/dairy/eggs were forbidden.
*   **Whale/Seal:** Also historically classified as fish.
*   **Yak:** Tibetan favorite; used for milk cheese, butter, and tea (butter tea).

# Pâté de Fruit (Pectin Gels)

### Problem: Scorching at High Temperatures
*   **Issue:** Fruit puree burning/browning at 180°F (82°C) and 225°F (107°C) during reduction.
*   **Root Cause:** Likely heat source issues (open flame vs. induction) rather than the temperature itself.
*   **Solution:**
    *   **Equipment:** Use an **induction burner** for precise temperature control to prevent scorching.
    *   **Technique:** Cook slowly.
    *   **Feasibility:** 225°F is safe for pectin gelation if temperature control is accurate; water removal is the goal, not just heat.
*   **Alternative Method (Experimental):**
    *   Use a **Rotovap** (rotary evaporator) to evaporate water at lower temperatures under vacuum.
    *   **Requirement:** Pectin requires boiling temperatures to activate *unless* using specific modified pectins (e.g., Lomethoxyamidated pectin).
    *   **Process:** Boil to dissolve pectin, then use Rotovap to remove water to target solids content.

# Resources & References

### Books & Authors
*   **Douglas Baldwin:** Mathematician; author of *Sous Vide Primer* and upcoming book on sous vide. Provides tables for calculating time-to-temperature based on food dimensions.
    *   *Note:* Formulas like "minutes per lb" are often inaccurate as cooking time is non-linear with size.
*   **Nathan Myhrvold:** Mathematician/Engineer; co-author of upcoming technology cookbook. Published foundational time/temperature tables on *eGullet*.
*   **Baruch Sharifi ("The Saffron King"):** Supplier of Mana candy and products.

### Products & Ingredients
*   **Mana:** Wild, sweet substance (dried sap or honeydew from bugs).
    *   **Characteristics:** Chewy, breaks easily, unique flavor profile per bite.
    *   **Candy:** Mana mixed with sugar and nuts.
    *   **Usage:** Delicious mixed with bourbon.
*   **Pectin:** Hydrocolloid requiring acidity and high soluble solids (sugar) to gel.
*   **Equipment:**
    *   **Immersion Circulator:** For precise water bath temperatures.
    *   **Rotovap:** For low-temp evaporation.
    *   **Induction Burner:** For precise stovetop heat control.
