### Sous Vide Techniques

**Ribs**
* **Recommended Temp:** 60°C (140°F) for a slightly pink interior.
* **Temperature Range:** 44°C (rare/not recommended) up to 80°C (traditional).
* **Timing & Texture:**
    * **24 Hours:** Texture similar to skirt steak.
    * **48+ Hours:** Texture similar to short ribs.
* **Advantage:** Meat remains on the bone and does not shred compared to traditional high-heat methods.

**Pork Chops (High-Fat/Shoulder Cuts)**
* **The "Hammy" Fat Problem:** Low temperatures do not render fat effectively, leading to an unappealing mouthfeel.
* **Technique: "Low Temperature for Insurance"**
    1. Cook to target internal temperature (e.g., 50s–60s°C).
    2. Chill the meat completely.
    3. Finish in a blazing hot oven to render fat and brown the exterior without overcooking the center.
* **Pre-searing:** Recommended for improved flavor and faster final browning.
* **Food Safety:** At low temperatures (around 54°C), crowded baths can shield meat, allowing lactic acid bacteria to grow (resulting in "sauerkraut" or "blue cheese" smells). 
    * **Prevention:** Quickly dunk the vacuum bag in simmering water to kill surface bacteria before starting the sous vide bath.

**General Parameters**
* **Braising Temp:** Dave typically uses 60°C; notes that benefits of low-temp cooking diminish above 63–64°C.

### Herb Preservation & Aromatics

**Cilantro Storage**
* **Fresh Preservation:** Cut stems, place in a glass of water, cover with a plastic bag secured by a rubber band, and refrigerate.
* **Drying:** Dry as gently as possible to minimize cell damage. Use breathable bags rather than tight wraps.
* **Aromatics:** Cilantro compounds are highly volatile. Mechanical damage (e.g., pounding for pesto) can actually mute the flavor.

**Advanced Aroma Preservation**
* **Liquid Nitrogen:** Can preserve aromatics, but ruptured tissues cause herbs to turn black upon thawing.
    * **Fix:** Blanch in boiling water and shock in ice before freezing.
* **Distillation:** Water-based distillations lose aromatics quickly. Dave suggests using a "cold finger" with liquid nitrogen during distillation and immediately melting the result into alcohol to lock in flavors.

### Carbonation & Dissolved Gases

**Gas Properties**
* **$\text{CO}_2$:** Highly soluble in water, alcohol, and fats. Provides sharp carbonation but can taste sour.
* **$\text{N}_2\text{O}$ (Nitrous Oxide):** Highly soluble; tastes slightly sweet. Used to balance $\text{CO}_2$ to create a "lively" but mellow mouthfeel.
* **$\text{N}_2$ (Nitrogen):** Relatively insoluble. 
    * **Beer Gas:** A mix of $\text{CO}_2$ and $\text{N}_2$ used to maintain high keg pressure for flow rate without increasing the $\text{CO}_2$ bubble load.
    * **Guinness Widget:** Uses $\text{N}_2$ to create a creamy head without adding $\text{CO}_2$.

### Ice Cream & Foams

**Salep (Orchid Powder)**
* **Function:** Creates "stretchy" ice cream.
* **Ratio:** 0.5% to 1% by weight.
* **Technique:** Must be frozen and then manually beaten to create a gluten-like network.
* **Equipment Warning:** Does **not** work in a Pacojet, as the chopping action destroys the required network.

**Olive Brine Foam**
* **Ingredients:** 
    * Granulated Lecithin: 0.5%–0.75%
    * Xanthan Gum: $\sim 0.25\%$
* **Technique:** Use a stick blender held at an angle to pull air into the liquid.

**Creme Anglaise Whipping**
* **Problem:** Cannot be whipped even when chilled.
* **Cause:** The cooking process denatures proteins. Additionally, egg proteins and sugars coat the oil droplets in the cream, preventing them from bonding into a foam.
* **Note on Cream:** Heated cream must be chilled for a significant period to allow fat crystals to reorganize before it can be whipped effectively.

### Roasting & Stocks

**Chicken Roasting**
* **Technique:** High-temp roasting (e.g., 400°F+) works well for small birds if the meat is brined and then **air-dried** (e.g., overnight in the fridge). 
* **Functional Role of Drying:** Creates a pellicle on the skin, allowing it to brown more easily and avoid sogginess.
* **Resting:** Essential after high-heat cooking to allow juices to redistribute from the exterior to the interior.

**Stock Water**
* **Distilled Water:** No detectable advantage for standard stocks; the water ceases to be distilled the moment ingredients are added.
* **Exception:** Hard water can negatively impact very delicate, short-duration extractions, such as Japanese kombu stocks.

### Other Culinary Science

**Ultrasound Marbling**
* **Application:** Used on live cattle since the 1980s to measure fat content.
* **Mechanism:** Ultrasound waves travel at different speeds through lean muscle versus fat, allowing for the prediction of prime marbling percentages.
