# Culinary Notes: Cooking Issues

## Cocktail Science
### Stirring vs. Shaking
* **Shaking:** 
    * Style has minimal impact on final temperature or dilution. 
    * Target duration: 12–15 seconds to reach equilibrium.
* **Stirring:** 
    * Less efficient for chilling than shaking.
    * Requires significantly more time (up to a minute or longer) to reach equilibrium.
* **Fundamental Principle:** Chilling and dilution are inextricably linked; you cannot achieve one without the other. Most chilling/dilution occurs via the melting of ice.

## Food Chemistry: Flavor & Storage
### Flavor Changes Over Time
* **Negative Effects (Oxidation):** 
    * Polyunsaturated fats in meats and potatoes can oxidize during storage/reheating, creating "cardboardy" or "warmed-over" flavors. 
    * **Mitigation:** Use vacuum bags to prevent oxygen exposure.
* **Positive Effects (Texture & Concentration):**
    * **Pizza:** Reheating can recrisp a crust by allowing migrated water to evaporate.
    * **Lasagna:** Pasta reabsorbs water from the sauce during cooling, concentrating the sauce flavor.
* **Positive Effects (Volatiles):**
    * Cooling and reheating causes high-note volatiles to "even out."
    * During refrigeration, volatile compounds undergo degradation or hydrolysis, creating a more complex, leveled-off flavor profile.

## Freezing & Thawing
### Freezing Techniques
* **The Physics of Freezing:** Freezing is fundamentally a dehydration process as water crystallizes out of cells.
* **Drip Loss:** High drip loss (liquid leaking during thaw) is a sign of poor quality/improper freezing due to cell damage.
* **Rapid Freezing Methods:**
    * **Liquid Nitrogen:** Minimizes drip loss. To prevent food from becoming too brittle, suspend it *above* the liquid to pre-freeze before full immersion.
    * **Salt Slush:** For home use, create a slush of ice and ~25% salt by weight. Submerge food in a bag within this slush for rapid freezing with small ice crystals.

### Thawing Techniques
* **Physics Note:** Ice is a better heat conductor than water. Thawing is slower than freezing because the exterior thaws into water first, which conducts heat less efficiently than ice.
* **Recommended Method:** Place food in a bag and submerge in **running water**. This is the fastest and gentlest method for even heat transfer.

## Sauce Stabilization
* **Non-Stable Ingredients:** Gelatin and agar are *not* freeze-thaw stable; they tend to "weep" (syneresis) upon thawing.
* **Stabilizers/Thickeners:** To prevent weeping in frozen sauces or gels, use:
    * **Xanthan Gum:** Acts as a stabilizer to stop weeping.
    * **Locust Bean Gum:** Requires heat to activate.
    * **Guar Gum:** Use high-quality, flavor-free versions (e.g., TIC gums).

## Heat & Capsaicin Management
### Removing Heat from Peppers (e.g., Habaneros)
* **Goal:** Remove capsaicin while preserving delicate floral aromas.
* **Manual Method:** Meticulously remove the interior veins, seeds, and the inner layer of skin.
* **Distillation Method:** 
    * Macerate/grind peppers in vodka.
    * Perform simple stovetop distillation (using a pot, ice, and a bowl). 
    * *Note:* Stovetop distillation reaches ~80°C, which may degrade floral aromas more than a Rotovap (~40°C).

## Meat Experiments (Odd Meats)
### Cooking Parameters & Profiles
* **Lion:** Tastes like savory pork. 
    * *Parameters:* Cooked at 57–60°C. Older animals require much longer cook times (up to 10 hours) to tenderize.
* **Black Bear:** Dark meat with a strong metallic/mineral aftertaste.
* **Raccoon:** High fat content; very tough if not cooked long enough.
    * *Parameters:* Recommended 63°C for 24 hours (shredding style).
* **Beaver (Tail):** Delicious, "woody" pulled-pork flavor.
    * *Parameters:* Soak in vinegar/water, salt, then sous vide at 60°C in butter for 24 hours.
* **Beaver (Flapper/Fat):** Mainly fat and skin.
    * *Technique:* Render at ~80°C, then fry to create a "chicharron" texture.
* **Yak:** Savory; notes of duck or tuna.
    * *Parameters:* Cook overnight (24 hours) at 57°C to achieve a texture similar to skirt steak.

## Grains: Farro
* **Techniques:**
    * **Soaking:** Highly recommended to soften the tough outer coat.
    * **Pressure Cooking:** Most effective for tough grains. Cook for 30–40 minutes in excess liquid.
    * **Risotto Style:** Fry the grains initially, then add liquid gradually while stirring.

## Caller Q&A
* **Q: Why do some foods (stews, sauces) taste better the next day?**
    * **A:** It is a combination of factors: sauce concentration via pasta absorption (lasagna), the leveling off of volatile compounds through hydrolysis/degradation during storage, and the deeper penetration of spices into the food.
* **Q: Can I put frozen meat directly into a crockpot?**
    * **A:** Yes. Because crockpots involve long, high-heat cooking, the water/juices will be extracted regardless. Searing from frozen is also acceptable.
* **Q: Is it dangerous to put your face in front of a microwave?**
    * **A:** Generally no. The metal grate is designed to block microwaves (light passes through because it has a shorter wavelength). However, do not press your eyes directly against the metal grate, and ensure the microwave is in good repair to avoid leakage from faulty latches.
* **Q: How do I cook farro so it is fluffy?**
    * **A:** Soak the grains first. Use a pressure cooker for 30–40 minutes with excess liquid. Alternatively, cook it "risotto style" by frying the grains and adding liquid gradually.

## Equipment & Recommendations
* **Pressure Cookers:** Highly recommended for grains and efficiency. **Kuhn Rikon** is a top-tier (though expensive) recommendation.
* **Microwave Detectors:** Useful for testing if a modified or old microwave is leaking radiation.
* **Rotovap:** The ideal tool for flavor extraction (like capsaicin removal) at low temperatures, though not always accessible for home use.
