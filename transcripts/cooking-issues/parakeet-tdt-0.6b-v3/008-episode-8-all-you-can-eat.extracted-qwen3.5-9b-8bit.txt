# Dorm Room Cooking Solutions
*   **Induction Burners:** Recommended despite potential RA restrictions. They do not heat unless a pan is present and pose no open flame hazard.
    *   *Strategy:* Persuade the RA that induction units are safe.
*   **Microwave/Toaster Oven Combinations:** Devices that look like microwaves but contain infrared heaters to function as toaster ovens.
*   **Westinghouse Turkey Oven:** 1950s countertop oven resembling an overgrown crock pot.
    *   *Source:* Thrift shops or eBay (approx. $2).
    *   *Function:* Cooks turkey while freeing up the main oven; safe enough to bake bread in a dorm.
*   **Sous Vide (Immersion Circulator):**
    *   *Function:* Heats liquid to accurate temperatures for repeatable results in small spaces.
    *   *Equipment:*
        *   *Commercial:* PolyScience (available at Williams Sonoma, ~$800).
        *   *DIY:* Build your own for $50–$200.
        *   *Components:* Immersion coffee heaters (3–5 units for ~1000W), aquarium pump, temperature controller with thermocouple.
        *   *Supplier:* Auber Instruments (Auber.com).
        *   *Time:* 1 hour for experienced builders; 4 hours for beginners.
        *   *Warning:* DIY units may leak electricity (shock hazard).
    *   *Packaging:* Ziplock bags (vacuum sealer not required).
*   **Microwave Searing Plate:**
    *   *Materials:* Silicon carbide sharpening stones + insulating fire bricks (or Corning ware).
    *   *Process:* Place stones in microwave, insulate, run until glowing red hot (~10 mins).
    *   *Capability:* Sear meat, melt metal (metal smelting possible).
    *   *Safety:* Do not light dorm on fire.

# Sous Vide Pot Roast Parameters
*   **Sauce Preparation:** Over-reduce sauce *before* bagging. Meat releases juices during cooking, which would otherwise water down the sauce.
*   **Temperature Range:**
    *   *Low End:* 57°C (possible but less preferred).
    *   *Happy Medium:* 60°C – 62°C (retains moisture, slight pinkness, traditional braise texture).
    *   *High End:* Up to 71°C (loses some low-temp benefits, but retains more moisture than traditional braising).
    *   *Avoid:* 65°C+ (squeezes out too much water).
*   **Time Recommendations:**
    *   *Short Rib:* 60°C for 48–50 hours.
    *   *General Rule:* 60°C–63°C for approximately 2 days (48 hours).
    *   *Adjustment for Grass-Fed Beef:* May require slightly longer time (e.g., 2 days at 60°C) to achieve softness compared to conventional beef.
*   **Flavor Enhancement:** Add roasted scrap meat to the bag for high-temperature flavor notes.

# Ingredient Safety & Testing
*   **Heritage Chicken (Sulfur/Rotting Smell):**
    *   *Cause:* Blood in the bag or age.
    *   *Action:* If the smell is strong/rotten, do not serve to others.
    *   *Test:* Rinse briefly in water/vinegar; smell again.
    *   *Rule:* If it makes you nervous, cook and eat it yourself first. Do not serve to friends/family if unsure.
*   **Bear Meat (Trichinosis):**
    *   *Risk:* Trichinosis (worms in muscle).
    *   *Mitigation:* Cooking kills the parasite.
    *   *Advice:* Eat if safe and cooked properly.

# Sauce Storage & Reheating
*   **Method:** Vacuum seal sauces in freezer-grade, oxygen-impermeable bags.
*   **Benefits:** Prevents water loss/free burn; allows direct reheating in hot water bath without scorching.
*   **Capacity:** Multiple bags can be reheated simultaneously in the same water bath.
*   **Emulsions:** Freezing is possible if stabilized with stabilizers; requires experimentation to prevent breaking.
*   **Application:** Excellent for large quantities and flat storage.

# Tomato Selection & Evaluation
*   **Varieties:**
    *   *Aunt Ruby's German Green:* Large, green, pinkish-red blush at the bottom when ripe.
    *   *German Stripe:* Red/yellow stripes.
    *   *Everlast:* Resembles Aunt Ruby's but is more yellow.
*   **Ripeness Check:** Inspect the **bottom** of the tomato first (where it detached from the plant). Look for softening and pink blush there before the top.
*   **Quality Control:**
    *   Heirloom tomatoes vary wildly by farmer/soil/location; "heirloom" label alone is insufficient.
    *   Avoid farmers who squeeze/mangle tomatoes to check ripeness.
    *   Specific farmer recommendation is necessary (e.g., Stokes Farm).

# Cocktail Physics: Ice & Dilution
*   **Fundamental Rule:** "There is no chilling without dilution, and there is no dilution without chilling."
*   **Ice Size vs. Temperature:**
    *   *Large Ice:* Less dilution, but temperature rises faster (e.g., from -4°C to ~5°C).
    *   *Small Ice:* More dilution, maintains lower temperature longer (e.g., -4°C to -0.5°C).
*   **Shaking vs. Stirring:**
    *   *Shaking:* Violent action reaches equilibrium quickly (12–15 seconds). Technique differences (straining, pushing gate) matter more than ice size.
    *   *Stirring:* Slower equilibrium (1–2 minutes). Technique (speed, duration, ice size) significantly affects final dilution and temperature.
    *   *Equilibrium Test:* Stirring to a specific temperature (e.g., -0.6°C) yields identical dilution regardless of time taken (30s vs. 1min).
*   **Freezer Ice vs. Counter Ice:**
    *   *Temperature Drop:* Freezer ice only chills the drink ~0.5°C more than counter ice (0°C).
    *   *Chilling Speed:* Counter ice (0°C) may chill a drink faster than freezer ice (-20°C) due to energy transfer dynamics (melting is a large energy sink).
    *   *Duration:* Freezer ice warms to 0°C very quickly (<20 mins) in a drink; does not maintain sub-zero temps long.
*   **Recommendation:** If shaking and serving on the rocks, use the same ice used for shaking (with surface water removed) to avoid extra dilution from surface water on fresh ice.
