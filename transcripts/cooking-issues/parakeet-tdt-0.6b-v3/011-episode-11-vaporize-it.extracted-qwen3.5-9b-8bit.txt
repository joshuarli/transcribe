# Wild Food Foraging
*   **Recommended Reading:**
    *   *Stalking the Wild Asparagus* and *Stalking the Wild Scallop* by **Yule Gibbons** (late 1960s/early 1970s).
    *   **Paul Stamets** website for mushroom guides (authorities on fungus).
*   **Safety & Technique:**
    *   **Identification:** Beginners must distinguish between edible and poisonous look-alikes. Mistakes can be fatal.
    *   **Local Knowledge:** Foraging guides are often dated; what is safe in one region may be poisonous in another.
    *   **Best Practice:** Do not collect mushrooms or wild plants alone for the first time. Join local mycological societies for guided tours.
    *   **Experience:** The safest method is to learn from an experienced forager who can teach identification by sight, touch, and smell in the field.
*   **Specific Ingredients & Locations:**
    *   **Lamb's Quarters (*Chenopodium album*):** A wild green growing in vacant lots in New York City (e.g., Lower East Side).
    *   **Sea Rocket:** A wild, spicy, slightly bitter green found in Maine (similar to arugula).
    *   **Sea Beans:** Salty wild greens found in Maine (reduce need for added salt).
    *   **Seaside Lamb's Quarters:** A seaside variant of the inland green.
    *   **Mussels and Sea Urchins:** Harvested from water in Maine.
    *   **Oklahoma:** Unknown specific wild ingredients, but local foraging is guaranteed to yield something.

# Extracting Pine Needle and Bark Flavors
*   **Extraction Methods:**
    *   **Alcohol:** Pine needle flavor extracts extremely well into alcohol using a vacuum machine.
    *   **Oil:** Feasible but untested by the host; requires caution regarding flavor profile.
    *   **Water:** Can be extracted using a vacuum machine.
    *   **Equipment:** Vacuum concentrator (Rotovap) or vacuum sealer/whipped cream maker (ISI whipper).
*   **Flavor Profile & Risks:**
    *   **Pine Varieties:** Different pines offer different profiles (e.g., sprucey vs. "duck fur" flavors).
    *   **Psychological Barrier:** Many people associate pine with "Pine-Sol" (bathroom cleaner) or "Pine-Salt." This association can make delicious pine flavors unpalatable to some.
    *   **Yew Warning:** Yew trees look like pine but are poisonous.
    *   **Bark:** Wood extracts (e.g., oak water) are potent, sour, and barky. Results via vacuum machine are untested but likely potent.
*   **Preparation:**
    *   **Tea:** Classically made by boiling pine needles.
    *   **Oil:** Can be made by infusing oil with pine needles.

# Smoking vs. Vaporizing Equipment
*   **PolyScience Smoking Gun:**
    *   **Mechanism:** Combusts material (wood chips, herbs) to create smoke.
    *   **Usage:** Injects smoke into containers or vacuum chambers.
    *   **Flavor Profile:** Smoke particles settle out over time; flavor differs from continuous smoking. Useful for infusing aroma without direct combustion of food.
    *   **History:** Evolved from reverse-engineered handheld vacuum cleaners.
*   **Herbal Vaporizers (e.g., Volcano):**
    *   **Mechanism:** Heats a chamber to a temperature below combustion to vaporize material without burning it.
    *   **Flavor Profile:** Produces cleaner flavors with less tar/nasty chemicals compared to combustion.
    *   **Culinary Application:** Used by Grant Aikatz at *Linia* restaurant to infuse aromas (e.g., vaporizing herbs).
    *   **Portable Butane Vaporizers:** Cheaper alternatives to the Volcano; function similarly (vaporization vs. combustion).
*   **Comparison:** Combustion (Smoking Gun) vs. Vaporization (Volcano). They are not equivalent; vaporization offers a cleaner, less smoky profile.

# Sugar Reduction and Meringue Stabilization
*   **Maltodextrin:**
    *   **Function:** Bulking agent and caloric reducer.
    *   **Structure:** Short chains of glucose units (starch broken down).
        *   Longer chains = less sweet, more bland.
        *   Shorter chains (e.g., 2 glucose units = maltose) = sweeter.
    *   **Caloric Impact:** Has calories like starch, but used in smaller quantities due to high bulking power, resulting in reduced caloric intake.
    *   **Diabetic Friendly:** Does not cause insulin spikes (unlike sugar).
    *   **Density:** Extremely low density (fluffy). 1 lb can expand to the size of a bean bag chair.
    *   **Oil-to-Powder Conversion:** Specific tapioca maltodextrin (**EnsoRite** from National Starch Corporation) is used to turn oils into powders.
        *   **Mechanism:** Starch helix structure is hydrophobic inside (holds oil) and hydrophilic outside (dissolves in water/mouth).
        *   **Note:** Not all maltodextrins are suitable for this; EnsoRite is specific.
*   **Methylcellulose:**
    *   **Source:** Modified cellulose (not found in nature, but not "ungodly").
    *   **Properties:** Gels when heated; acts as a whipping agent.
    *   **Application:** Used in meringues to replace egg whites (protein) for structure.
    *   **Process:** Whip fruit purees (bulked with pectin/solids) + Methylcellulose + Sweetener (non-sugar) -> Dehydrate.
    *   **Limitation:** Highly hygroscopic (sucks up moisture); does not last long outside.
*   **Recipe Modification:**
    *   Combine maltodextrin (bulking) + Xanthan gum (thickening) + Methylcellulose (whipping) to create low-sugar meringues.

# Infused Vodka and Reduction Techniques
*   **Habanero Vodka:**
    *   **Green Habaneros:** Flavor is fairly stable over time.
    *   **Red Habaneros:** Flavor is unstable. Lasts 2–6 months before degrading to a "greener," jalapeño-like taste.
    *   **Storage:** Freezing extends shelf life.
    *   **Proof:** Stable at ~100 proof (50% alcohol).
    *   **Culinary Use:** Excellent in sauces; provides habanero aroma without the heat (capsaicin).
*   **Vodka Reduction in Sauces:**
    *   **Purpose:** Adding vodka releases volatile aromas.
    *   **Reduction:** Boiling removes some ethanol but not all.
    *   **Technique Question:** Why reduce vodka after adding it?
        *   **Host Opinion:** Reducing to remove alcohol is pointless if the goal is aroma release. Adding a smaller amount later might achieve the same result.
        *   **Hypothesis:** Reduction may be unnecessary for aroma release; timing of addition is key.

# Equipment and Anecdotes
*   **Vacuum Machine Transport:**
    *   **Event:** Mark Ladner (Del Posto) donated a 500 lb, partially functional vacuum machine to the French Culinary Institute.
    *   **Challenge:** Located 2.5 miles away (16th St to Hudson River); no lift gate for truck.
    *   **Solution:** Host pushed the machine manually on the hottest day of the year to avoid truck rental costs.
    *   **Philosophy:** Extreme cheapness and resourcefulness (e.g., biking to Brooklyn, buying cheap equipment) vs. spending on high-quality ingredients.
*   **Clarified Concord Grape Drink (Vogue Event):**
    *   **Process:**
        1.  Ice crush grapes.
        2.  Add **SPL** (pectin-breaking enzyme from CookingIssues.com).
        3.  Grind in chocolate grinder (set high to avoid crushing seeds, which cause bitterness).
        4.  Spin in centrifuge.
        5.  Add lemon juice and maple syrup.
        6.  Chill with liquid nitrogen and water.
    *   **Base:** Rye whiskey (for future version, switch to Maker's Mark).
    *   **Volume:** ~400 drinks sold in one hour.
