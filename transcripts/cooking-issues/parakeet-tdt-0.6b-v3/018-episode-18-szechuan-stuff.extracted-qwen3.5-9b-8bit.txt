# Vietnamese Egg Soda
*   **Technique:**
    *   Add sweetened condensed milk to the bottom of a glass.
    *   Crack an egg yolk into the milk and stir to combine.
    *   Top with soda water.
    *   Stir vigorously to create foam.
*   **Science/Properties:**
    *   **Cooking:** The egg yolk is **not cooked** by the soda water. Do not assume bacteria is killed by this method; use pasteurized eggs if serving pregnant individuals or those with immune issues.
    *   **Foaming:** Egg yolks contain proteins and emulsifiers (lessithin, phospholipids) that stabilize air bubbles in water emulsions, similar to egg whites but with different mechanisms.
    *   **Safety:** Salmonella contamination in eggs is rare (approx. 1 in 2,000 to 1 in 8,000).

# Sous Vide: Wagyu and Searing
*   **Wagyu vs. Standard Beef:**
    *   **Fat Rendering:** Wagyu fat (less saturated) may render at lower temperatures than standard beef, but significant rendering does not occur in the typical sous vide range (54°C to 57°C / approx. 130°F to 135°F).
    *   **Texture:** Highly marbled meat handles sous vide similarly to lower-grade cuts; the primary difference is the amount of internal fat, which keeps the meat juicy.
    *   **Philosophy:** Sous vide is excellent for lower-grade cuts to prevent drying out, but it does not turn a "crappy cut" into a high-quality cut.
*   **Searing Protocol (Pre- and Post-Sear):**
    *   **Recommendation:** Sear **both before** and **after** sous vide for optimal results.
    *   **Preference Data:** In blind taste tests, 75–80% of people prefer meat seared both before and after compared to searing only after.
    *   **Benefits of Pre-Sear:**
        *   Kills surface bacteria (safety).
        *   Initiates Maillard reaction (color and flavor) inside the bag.
        *   Creates a head start for crust formation.
    *   **Benefits of Post-Sear:**
        *   Creates a dark, flavorful crust (meat coming out of the bag has no crust).
    *   **Procedure:** Chill the meat after the pre-sear before placing it in the bag.

# Nitrous Oxide (ISI) Infusion
*   **Equipment:** ISI whipped cream chargers (nitrous oxide).
*   **Process:**
    1.  Place porous ingredient (e.g., cocoa nibs, star anise) and liquid (e.g., liquor) in a container.
    2.  Hit with nitrous oxide chargers.
    3.  Pressurize and let sit for 1–3 minutes (time depends on product).
    4.  Vent quickly to release pressure, causing gas to bubble out and extract flavor rapidly.
*   **Science:**
    *   Nitrous oxide is highly soluble in liquids, unlike nitrogen used in laboratory cavitation.
    *   Infusions often taste better after sitting (5–15 minutes) because the gas remains dissolved longer.
*   **Troubleshooting Cocoa Nibs:**
    *   **Raw Nibs:** Do not use raw, unroasted cocoa nibs; they taste acrid, sour, or like "a donkey's behind."
    *   **Toasting:** Nibs must be toasted.
    *   **Brand Recommendation:** Valrhona cocoa nibs yield the best results due to porosity.
    *   **Bar Chocolate:** Not recommended for this technique due to low porosity. Alternative: Melt chocolate with liquor, freeze, and separate fat (similar to fat washing) to extract bitterness.

# Sichuan Peppercorns (Tingling Effect)
*   **Goal:** Concentrate the numbing/tingling sensation (not the flavor).
*   **Distillation:** Likely **not possible**. The tingling sensation is not volatile enough to distill (unlike horseradish or wasabi pungency).
*   **Recommended Methods:**
    1.  **Tincture:** Infuse in high-proof alcohol.
    2.  **ISI Infusion:**
        *   Use high-proof liquor.
        *   Hit with 2 chargers.
        *   Swirl, let sit 1 minute, swirl again, let sit 1 minute.
        *   Vent, let stand 5 minutes, strain.
    3.  **Alternative Ingredient:** Sancho peppers (Japanese green peppercorns).
        *   Look for "double balls" (two green spheres inside).
        *   Flavor profile is intermediate between Sichuan peppercorns and Sichuan buttons (less aggressive than buttons).
*   **Sichuan Buttons:** Generally discouraged by the host; too aggressive ("tongue on a car battery") and long-lasting effects make them difficult to serve.

# Immersion Circulators (PolyScience)
*   **Model 71 (Old School Analog):**
    *   **Calibration:** Requires external digital thermometer and thermocouple. Dial until heating light stabilizes.
    *   **Over-temp Thermostat:** Located on the back. Default setting is too low for water baths.
    *   **Adjustment:** Turn up to 200°C (approx. 400°F) or higher to prevent clicking off during normal operation.
    *   **Maintenance:** Bearings may squeal after months/years; lubricate with WD-40. Motor failure is eventual.
*   **New Digital Models:**
    *   **NYC Compliance:** Sand off the "Sous Vide" label on the front plate to avoid health inspector issues.
    *   **Calibration Risk:** Users can accidentally change the offset (calibration) by pressing buttons, throwing the unit off by ~3°C (e.g., turning a perfect 62°C poached egg into a raw 59°C egg).
    *   **Feature:** Dual unit display (Fahrenheit/Celsius).

# Xanthan Gum
*   **Function:** Stabilizer and thickener for water-based systems; creates body.
*   **Usage Rates:** 0.25% to 0.4% (1/4 to 3/4 of 1%). Higher amounts cause a "snotty" texture.
*   **Limitations:**
    *   **Oil:** Xanthan gum **will not thicken oil**. It is a polysaccharide that does not interact with oil.
    *   **Agar Substitution:** Not a good straight substitute for agar gels.
*   **Oil Thickening Alternatives:**
    1.  **Mono- and Diglycerides:**
        *   Sold as flakes (e.g., Glec by Feron Texturas).
        *   Melts out when hot; best for cold preps.
        *   Requires high concentration (~10%) for spreadable texture, which tastes nasty.
    2.  **Emulsion:** Mix mono- and diglycerides with water, then emulsify into oil.
    3.  **Mayonnaise:** The best method to thicken smoked oil.

# Astringency Reduction
*   **Mechanism:** Dehydration (freezing or drying) can bond tannins with other compounds, reducing astringency.
*   **Fruits:** Persimmons and apples lose astringency when frozen/thawed or dried.
*   **Walnut Liquor:**
    *   **Freezing:** Hard to freeze high-proof liquor; may work if low-proof.
    *   **Source:** Astringency comes from walnut skins.
    *   **Alternatives:**
        *   Use walnut strains with low-tannin red skins.
        *   Skin walnuts before infusion (painful process).
        *   Try ISI infusion on cracked/smashed walnuts to extract flavor without tannins (though pecan infusions via ISI still yielded tannins).
        *   Liquid nitrogen freezing (dehydrates) followed by slow thaw.

# Clarification of Juices
*   **Agar:** Works on canned pineapple juice and bottled orange juice.
*   **Enzymatic Clarification:**
    *   Use Pectinex Smash XXL or Pectinex SPL.
    *   **Orange Juice:** Can be clarified directly enzymatically if not highly acidic.
    *   **Pineapple Juice:** Contains proteolytic enzymes that attack gelatin; agar is required (cooking the juice first deactivates enzymes).
