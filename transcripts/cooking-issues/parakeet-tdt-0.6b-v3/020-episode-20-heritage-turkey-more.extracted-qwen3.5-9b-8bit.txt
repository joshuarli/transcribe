# Thanksgiving & Heritage Turkey (Narragansett)
*   **Bird Selection:** Heritage breed (Narragansett), approx. 15 lbs.
*   **Preparation:**
    *   Boned inside out.
    *   Constructed an aluminum skeleton with leg bones cut to have holes drilled through them (sprinkler system).
    *   Hooked skeleton to a sous vide circulator.
    *   Piped hot oil through the leg bones to cook legs from the inside out.
*   **Cooking Parameters:**
    *   **Circulator Setting:** 60.75°C (141.35°F) target for final leg temperature.
    *   **Duration:** Approx. 1 hour 20 minutes in oil bath.
    *   **Fat Bath:** Transferred whole bird (still pumping) into duck fat.
    *   **Fat Temp Profile:** Started at 66°C, dropped to 65°C, finished at 64°C.
    *   **Cooling:** Cooled in fat (confit style).
*   **Finishing:**
    *   Oven finish at 500°C (932°F) with convection.
    *   Used a clay baking sheet (Rumford style) to brown the bottom.
    *   *Note:* Clay sheet shattered under high heat; drippings smoked heavily.
*   **Brining:** Light brine only.
*   **Result:** "Best damn turkey ever made," intense meaty flavor, no gravy needed.

# Hydrocolloid Course (FCI)
*   **Cost:** $1,500 (plus travel/hotel).
*   **Duration:** 2 days.
*   **Instructors:** Dave Arnold, Nils Noran.
*   **Philosophy:**
    *   Focus on principles and "how to think" rather than strict recipes.
    *   Goal: Enable students to develop new recipes independently using ingredients like sodium alginate and calcium carbonate.
    *   Rigorous approach to techniques (e.g., spherification) compared to general availability.
*   **Technical Content Covered:**
    *   **Sodium Alginate:** Seaweed-derived gelling agent.
        *   Forms gel upon contact with free calcium.
        *   Gel is heat-stable and permanent.
        *   Poor flavor profile (tastes "wretched").
        *   **Acidity Issue:** High acidity prevents setting.
        *   **Solutions for Acidity:** Use sequestrants (e.g., sodium hexametaphosphate) to bind freed calcium, or buffering agents (e.g., sodium citrate).
    *   **Flavor Development:** Alginate gels absorb flavor; never eat the gel itself.

# Spherification & Texture Modification
*   **Caller Question:** How to make alginate spheres (oyster soup) more pearlescent/shimmery and less "solidified snot" texture.
*   **Method 1: Visual Only (Not Recommended)**
    *   Add food-grade pearlescent powder or metal dust (e.g., silver leaf/powder) to the bath.
    *   Reference: Kike de Costa's technique (adding silver dust).
    *   *Tradeoff:* Improves look but adds no flavor benefit; potential flavor impact concerns.
*   **Method 2: Texture & Visual Improvement (Recommended)**
    *   **Technique:** Emulsify oil into the alginate base *before* dropping.
    *   **Mechanism:** Oil breaks up the solid gel structure, making it softer and easier to chew.
    *   **Visual Result:** Creates a "salad dressingy" shimmery look.
    *   **Critical Constraint:** Alginate must be the continuous phase (emulsify oil *into* alginate, not vice versa) to maintain integrity.

# Pectinex & Juice Reduction
*   **Application:** Clarified fruit juices (Apple, Strawberry, Blood Orange) reduced to syrups or used in candy bases.
*   **Process:**
    *   Clarify juice using Pectinex.
    *   Reduce in rotary evaporator (preferred to preserve flavor) or stovetop.
    *   **Example:** Reduced clarified strawberry juice to ~33% solids, stirred into cooled candy base.
*   **Heat Stability:** Pectinex is stable enough for reduction; some flavor cooking is acceptable.
*   **Sweet Potato Caramelization Idea:**
    *   **Goal:** Extract sugars from sweet potato starch to make caramel.
    *   **Proposed Method:**
        1.  Juice sweet potato immediately (to free enzymes).
        2.  Add brewer's alpha/beta amylase to convert starch to sugar (like brewing wort).
        3.  Clarify the liquid.
        4.  **Crucial Step:** Spin out/clarify starch *before* gelatinization occurs.
        5.  Cook down the clarified sugar liquid.
    *   **Alternative:** Treat pulp with Pectinex and re-juice.
    *   **Yield:** Juicing yields more liquid than chipping/canning.

# Whipped Cream Makers (EC/ISI)
*   **Recommendation:** Do not buy the "Seltzer" model; buy a "Cream" model.
*   **Models:** Profi Whip, Gourmet Whip, etc.
*   **Feature:** Newer models have silicone grip rings (older ones do not).
*   **Thermal Whip:**
    *   Insulated, smaller capacity (0.5L vs 0.75L standard).
    *   **Use Case:** Maintaining hot/cold products for service without fridge/bain-marie access.
    *   **Tradeoff:** Higher cost per unit, lower capacity, but cheaper per recipe run due to smaller volume.
*   **Scaling:** Recipes typically written for 0.5L; can scale up to 1L containers.
*   **Infusion Capability:**
    *   **Pineapple + Alcohol:** Works to infuse both liquid and fruit.
    *   **Comparison:** Less effective than vacuum infusion but close.
    *   **Caveat:** Must use Nitrous Oxide (N2O) chargers.
        *   Avoid CO2 chargers (causes carbonation in fruit).
        *   N2O prevents boiling off of alcohol during the process.
        *   Pineapple aroma is fleeting; do not store infused fruit long-term.

# Sous Vide Equipment
*   **Sous Vide Supreme:**
    *   **Type:** Non-stirred water bath cooker.
    *   **Accuracy:** Limited accuracy due to lack of circulation; relies on long periods to equalize.
    *   **Recommendation:** Not as accurate or even as an immersion circulator.
    *   **Price:** ~$200 less than a circulator.
    *   **Verdict:** Save the money for a high-quality immersion circulator.
    *   **DIY Alternative:** Preferred over buying the Supreme; can build a home circulator.
    *   **Hack:** Add an aquarium pump to a Sous Vide Supreme to circulate water and fix accuracy issues (~$25 solution).
*   **Immersion Circulators:**
    *   Accuracy: +/- 0.2°C across the bath.
    *   Versatility: Various sizes, space-efficient.

# Carbonation (Savory & Sweet)
*   **Equipment:** Twist & Sparkle (EC unit).
*   **Savory Applications (Sauces/Broths):**
    *   **Feasibility:** Possible but difficult.
    *   **Constraints:**
        *   Must be served cold (heating loses carbonation).
        *   Must taste good carbonated (CO2 can make savory dishes taste fermented).
        *   **Foaming Risk:**
            *   No milk products (foams over).
            *   No particles/solids (foams and sprays everywhere).
            *   Liquid must be clear.
    *   **Test:** Try a small batch (50ml) first.

# Dr. Pepper Infusion
*   **Caller Goal:** Infuse Dr. Pepper flavor into neutral grain spirit (distilled).
*   **Dave's Advice:**
    *   **Direct Infusion:** Buy Dr. Pepper syrup (high sugar concentration). Mix with liquor, dilute to drinking strength, carbonate. Fastest method.
    *   **Distillation/Clarification:**
        *   Clarifying syrup won't remove sugars/bittering agents effectively.
        *   Redistilling yields only aroma, not the full flavor profile.
        *   **Complex Approach:** Reverse engineer flavor profile (prunes, spices, acids).
        *   Use white prunes (e.g., Brignol) for dried fruit flavor without purple color.
        *   Add plum essence.
        *   Result: Clear liquid mimicking the flavor balance.
*   **Soda Consumption Note:** Diet soda drinkers use it as a hydration technique (multiple small sips) rather than for sugar.
