# Salt-Rising Bread

*   **Leavening Agent:** *Clostridium perfringens* (anaerobic bacteria).
    *   **Note:** This is a pathogen/foodborne illness bacteria. The vegetative cells are killed by baking, but spores survive. No reported cases of illness from the finished bread.
    *   **Mechanism:** Produces gases (hydrogen and hydrogen sulfide) rather than just carbon dioxide.
    *   **Aroma:** Characteristic "cheesy" smell due to butyric acid (smell of rancid butter, Parmesan cheese, or vomit).
*   **Starter Preparation:**
    *   **Base:** Liquid mix with cornmeal (or similar feed).
    *   **Additives:** Salt, baking soda, or Campden tablets (yeast inhibitor).
    *   **Goal:** Inhibit yeast; allow only *C. perfringens* to grow.
    *   **Temperature:** Maintain at ~100°F (38°C).
    *   **Equipment:** Excalibur dehydrator (used as a proofer).
    *   **Duration:** Overnight (until foamy and aromatic).
*   **Dough Process:**
    *   Add water and flour to the spongy starter.
    *   Let rise again for many hours.
    *   Bake.
*   **Texture/Appearance:** Very white crumb, finer bubbles, distinct texture from standard bread.
*   **Dave's Experiment Notes:**
    *   **Issue:** Bread was under-proofed (thrown in oven too early).
    *   **Result:** Under-risen, no crust treatment (no egg wash).
    *   **Post-Bake Handling:** Immediately placed into a metal box after removal from oven.
    *   **Future Plans:** Experiment with alternative grains (rye) and longer proofing times.

# Sous Vide & Low-Temperature Cooking

## Brisket / Corned Beef
*   **Texture Goal:** Equated to short rib texture.
*   **Temperature Threshold:** Do not cook below 57°C (135°F).
    *   **Reasoning:** Below 57°C, meat remains too rare/bloody for most palates.
    *   **Safety Note:** If cooking below 57°C, perform a quick dip in simmering water (15–30 seconds) to kill surface bacteria before bagging, OR pre-sear. Prevents lactic acid bacteria growth if bags touch.
*   **Time/Temperature Ranges:**
    *   **57°C:** 48–60 hours.
    *   **60°C (140°F):** ~48 hours (Dave's preference for "beefier" flavor).
    *   **63–64°C:** Shorter time.
    *   **>64°C:** Cook traditionally (higher temp, shorter time).
    *   **Warning:** >72 hours at low temps results in mushy texture (loss of bite).
*   **Bagging Instructions:**
    *   **Spices:** Apply spices directly to meat; reduce quantity slightly if vacuum sealing tightly.
    *   **Liquid:** Minimize liquid in the bag to avoid a "poached" taste. Reduce any liquid added.

## Pork Shanks
*   **Comparison:** 70°C for 36 hours is roughly equivalent to 61°C for 48 hours.
*   **Recommendation:** Cook at 61°C for 48 hours OR 63°C for 56–57 hours.

## Beer Cooler Sous Vide (Whole Fish)
*   **Safety:** Safe to cook whole fish in a beer cooler.
*   **Challenge:** Temperature drop due to thermal mass of meat.
*   **Procedure:**
    1.  **Calibration:** Drill a hole for a long-stem thermometer. Fill with water, measure cooling rate over 1.5–2 hours. Target stability within 5–10°C drop.
    2.  **Strike Temperature Calculation:** Calculate initial water temperature based on meat weight, meat starting temp, desired final temp, and water volume.
        *   *Analogy:* Use brewer's "strike water temperature" calculator (e.g., brewheads.com).
        *   *Example:* If cooking at 57°C (135°F) for 1.5 hours with a 10°C drop, start water at ~145°F (63°C).
        *   *Formula Logic:* `(Water Mass × Water Temp) + (Meat Mass × Meat Temp) = Total Mass × Target Temp`.
*   **Equipment:** Standard 5-gallon homebrew cooler (e.g., Geo TT).

## Chicken Breast (Ziploc Bag)
*   **Liquid Usage:** Adding liquid leaches flavor into the bag.
*   **Reuse Strategy:**
    *   **Stock:** Strain and freeze stock. Repeated freeze/thaw cycles do not diminish quality if oxygen exposure is minimized and fat content is low.
    *   **Alternative (Dave's Method):** Add a small amount of milk (just enough to seal the bag) + sugar (as a brine substitute).
        *   **Result:** Liquid becomes a salty soup base (diluted chicken flavor).
*   **Steak:**
    *   **Do Not Add Water/Stock:** Avoids dilution.
    *   **Recommended Additive:** Melted butter + chopped garlic.
    *   **Post-Cook Sauce:** Strain garlic from butter. Whisk meat juices into the butter. Pour over garlic bread and toast.

# Equipment & Tools

## B. Braun Thermomix (Thermal Mixer)
*   **Description:** Old-school European circulator (blender/heater/mixer/scale). Distinct from modern Vitamix-style thermal mixers.
*   **Status:** Discontinued; hard to find new units.
*   **Maintenance:**
    *   **Failure Mode:** Bearing failure (unsealed bearings from the 80s).
    *   **Fix:** Lubricate bearings with WD-40 (non-food grade, but effective for extending life).
    *   **Calibration:** Check against ice water bath (should read 0°C).
    *   **Cleaning:** Can be difficult if oil baths were used previously.
*   **Dave's Unit:** 1419 model, "B. Braun Green," built in early 80s, acquired from a naval salvage at Pearl Harbor.

## AeroPress
*   **Dave's Experience:** Mixed results.
*   **Issue:** Produces a flat cup.
*   **Cause:** Paper filters restrict oils/flavor.
*   **Recommendation:** Use a metal filter to improve extraction and flavor profile.
*   **Comparison:** Similar technique to Clover machines (piston extraction), but AeroPress theoretically generates slight pressure.

## Coffee Brewing
*   **Preference:** Clover machine (independent coffee shop) preferred over AeroPress for flavor extraction.

# Ingredients & Functional Roles

*   **Salt in Salt-Rising Bread:**
    *   Function: Inhibits yeast formation (allows bacterial leavening).
    *   Quantity: Does not require high salt content (this specific loaf was undersalted).
*   **Butter in Steak Bag:**
    *   Function: Flavor carrier; creates a sauce base when combined with meat juices.
    *   Usage: Melted with garlic, cooked with steak, strained, and used for garlic bread.
*   **Sugar in Chicken Bag:**
    *   Function: Brine substitute.
*   **Milk in Chicken Bag:**
    *   Function: Sealant (minimal liquid needed).
*   **Barley Malt:**
    *   **Kilning:** Typically kilned to ~31°C (Dave notes this might be too high for "raw" beer concepts).
    *   **Alternative:** Use unmalted/sprouted grain (e.g., corn) for raw beer experiments.

# Caller Q&A

**Caller: Norm (Seattle)**
*   **Question:** Seeking pointers for using a restored B. Braun Thermal Mixer to cook a cured corned beef brisket.
*   **Answer:**
    *   **Equipment Check:** Verify calibration with ice water. Lubricate bearings if squealing/grinding.
    *   **Cooking Method:** Treat like short rib. Target 60°C (140°F) for ~48 hours for optimal texture/flavor. Avoid going below 57°C without surface sterilization (simmer dip or sear).
    *   **Bagging:** Apply spices directly; minimize liquid to prevent poaching effect.

**Caller: Mike (Brewer)**
*   **Question:** Feasibility of brewing "raw beer" using saliva amylase (chewing starch) instead of enzymatic mashing, avoiding high temps that kill enzymes.
*   **Answer:**
    *   **Feasibility:** Theoretically possible but problematic.
    *   **Issues:**
        *   Enzyme profiles differ from commercial malt enzymes; conversion may be incomplete.
        *   Cannot boil the wort (kills enzymes).
        *   Sterile filtration is difficult (filters clog).
        *   Lactobacillus thrives at low temps (11–18°C), causing souring before starch conversion.
        *   Barley is typically kilned too high for raw processes.
    *   **Suggestion:** Investigate using unmalted/sprouted corn or alternative grains.
