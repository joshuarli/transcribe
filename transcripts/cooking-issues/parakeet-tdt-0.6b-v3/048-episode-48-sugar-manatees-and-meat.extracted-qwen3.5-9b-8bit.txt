# Sugar Science & Melting Points
*   **Core Concept:** Sugar (sucrose) does not have a traditional melting point; it decomposes and melts simultaneously.
*   **Research Source:** University of Illinois research group.
*   **Decomposition Temperature:** Sugar starts to break down roughly at **120°C**.
*   **Heating Rate Dependency:** The apparent melting point shifts based on heating speed.
    *   Example: Ramping temperature at **5600°C per minute** still shows a shift in the melting/decomposition point.
*   **Implication for Sous Vide:** **120°C** is too hot for standard sous vide applications.
*   **Implication for Pressure Cookers:**
    *   At **120°C** and normal pressure, sugar takes a very long time to start breaking down.
    *   Temperatures above **120°C** can be achieved in a pressure cooker by reducing the amount of water present.

# Sorrel (Hibiscus Style)
*   **Acidity Profile:** Contains **oxalic acid** (partially), giving it an acidity similar to rhubarb.
*   **Flavor Profile:**
    *   *Raw:* Bright, fresh, acidic.
    *   *Cooked:* Develops a "cooked spinach" flavor that muddles the freshness.
*   **Texture:** Becomes fibrous/goopy when cooked; can be strained through cheesecloth to remove fibers for a creamy consistency.
*   **Technique for Combining Flavors:** Mix fresh sorrel with cooked sorrel to retain brightness while utilizing cooked texture (similar to mixing fresh and cooked blueberries in pies).
*   **Alternative Brightness:** Make a tea from dried hibiscus (red sorrel) and mix it back into the dish to restore brightness.
*   **Color Changes in Greens:**
    *   Darkening is often due to loss of top notes/aromatics, enzymatic browning, or chlorophyll turning olive green over long cooking times.
    *   Blanching whole vegetables quickly can help preserve green color.
    *   Rotary evaporation on basil results in a "cooked spinach" smell due to specific compounds (exact compounds not recalled by Dave).

# Salt and Bitterness
*   **Phenomenon:** Adding salt to bitter foods (e.g., grapefruit, tonic water, coffee) reduces perceived bitterness.
*   **Mechanism:**
    *   Salt acts on the **ionic strength** of the solution.
    *   Salt modifies how bitter molecules are perceived at the **taste receptor sites** (not just a cognitive brain effect).
    *   Salt must be at the same site as the bitter compound to alter perception.
*   **Limitations:** Salt affects some bitter compounds but not all (e.g., one specific compound was unaffected in testing).
*   **Reference:** "The influence of sodium salts on binary mixtures of bitter tasting compounds" by Keist.

# Sous Vide Fish & Vacuum Packaging
*   **Vacuum Sealing Risks:**
    *   Applying too much vacuum can negatively affect fish texture.
    *   Vacuum packaging can lead to faster loss of fresh fish flavor intensity and visual deterioration compared to Modified Atmosphere Packaging (MAP) with CO2, even if oxidation (TBARS) is lower.
    *   Residual oxygen in vacuum bags can still allow microbial growth.
    *   **Recommendation:** Do not store fish for extended periods in vacuum bags if maximum quality is the goal. Cook and eat soon after purchase.
*   **Packaging Methods:**
    *   **Plastic Wrap:** Wrap fish in multiple layers of plastic wrap. Can cook directly in the wrap (rolling technique).
    *   **Ziploc Bags:** A good alternative that avoids over-vacuuming texture issues.
*   **Monkfish Cooking Parameters:**
    *   *Option A (Low Temp/Fudgy):* **60°C (140°F)** for **12–15 minutes** (Target core temp ~48°C).
    *   *Option B (Traditional/Firm):* **57°C (135°F)** for **30 minutes**.
    *   *Option C (Longer):* **48°C** for **1 hour** (Dave notes this might be too long and cause mushiness due to enzymes).
    *   *Dave's Preference:* Likely closer to **50°C**, avoiding the full hour to prevent breakdown.
*   **Enzyme Concern:** Fish enzymes can break down tissue if cooked too long, resulting in a mushy texture.

# Cookie Creaming Technique
*   **Purpose:** Beating fat (butter) and sugar together adds air to the mixture, creating volume and a light, fluffy texture.
*   **Equipment:** Requires a mixer (stand mixer or immersion circulator); cannot be effectively done with a fork.
*   **Order of Operations:**
    1.  Cream fat and sugar to create air bubbles.
    2.  Add eggs (one at a time) to increase volume and evenness of the mix.
    3.  Add flour last.
*   **Impact of Order:**
    *   Adding eggs before flour helps incorporate flour without developing gluten (important for cookies).
    *   Adding eggs halfway through flour addition generally does not matter much for cookies unless significant aeration is desired (unlike cakes).
    *   Texture range: Melting butter creates dense/fudgy brownies; creaming creates lighter textures.

# 3D Printed Meat (Organovo)
*   **Company:** Organovo.
*   **Current Capability:** Grows whole blood vessels and human tissue (muscle, etc.) for drug testing (better than mouse/pig tissue).
*   **Future Potential:** Printing undifferentiated cells that bind, differentiate, and form actual organs or meat with fat marbling.
*   **Scientific Challenges:**
    *   Replicating the enzyme makeup of aged animals (e.g., older pigs have different enzyme profiles for ham than younger pigs).
    *   Accounting for diet and age effects on muscle breakdown.
*   **Dave's Opinion:** Current meat substitutes are often "overcooked, horrible, mono-textural pablum." True 3D printed meat with marbling is the closest attempt to real food but remains scientifically complex.
*   **Goal:** Cruelty-free manatee steak (referencing the extinction of the Stellar sea cow due to being "delicious" and having no predators).
