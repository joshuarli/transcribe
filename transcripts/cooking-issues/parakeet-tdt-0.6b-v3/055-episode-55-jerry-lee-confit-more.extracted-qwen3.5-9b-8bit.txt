# Techniques & Parameters

### Enzymatic Peeling & Clarification
*   **Technique:** Enzymatic peeling of fruits to remove albedo (white pith) and auto-supreming.
*   **Application:** Self-clarification of liquids (e.g., apple juice).
*   **Equipment/Ingredient:** Centerfuge required for strawy purees (though a workaround exists).
*   **Enzyme:** Pectanex SPL (ultra enzyme).
*   **Process:** Mix alginate with calcium (calcium gluconate or calcium lactate recommended over chloride for flavor) to form a gel. Drip/dip into calcium bath to set.

### Centrifugation
*   **Goal:** Clarification (e.g., using Pectanex SPL).
*   **Target Force:** ~4,000 G's.
*   **Capacity:** 3–4 L benchtop centrifuge preferred.
    *   *Reasoning:* Spins ~3 L at a time; allows spinning two buckets simultaneously (one with product, one with sugar as weight) for balance.
    *   *Avoid:* Small units (e.g., 1.5 L capacity) requiring excessive batches.
*   **Temperature Management:** Refrigeration helpful but not strictly necessary for 1–2 batches. Continuous use generates heat via friction; products can heat up drastically.
*   **Safety:** Calculate G-force based on rotor and RPM using online calculators.
*   **Cost:** New units ~$8,000; used/refurbished ~$1,000–$1,200; Dave's unit acquired for <$100 + shipping via misspelled eBay auction.

### Tincture Infusion (Rapid vs. Traditional)
*   **Rapid Infusion:** Accelerates infusion by ~10%.
*   **Time/Pressure Regimes:**
    *   *Rapid:* 1–5 minutes.
    *   *Extended:* 8 hours to overnight (under pressure) to mimic traditional percolation and air displacement.
*   **Alcohol Proof:**
    *   *US Limit:* 51% ABV (100 proof).
    *   *Higher Proof:* Requires travel to NJ/CT for Everclear or sourcing lab/perfume grade (195 proof USP).
    *   *Flavor Note:* Higher proof extracts differently; ratios of compounds may vary.
*   **Outcome:** Rapid infusion yields different flavor profiles than traditional steeping; testing required.

### Confit vs. Steaming
*   **Theory:** Fat acts as a gentle, low-temperature liquid medium (dry cooking medium) that prevents evaporation loss compared to roasting. It does not significantly add flavor or moisture.
*   **Test Method:**
    1.  Use a combi oven.
    2.  Run one duck in steam at 85°C.
    3.  Run one duck in traditional fat confit in the same cavity, same time, same temperature.
    4.  Compare results.
*   **Conclusion:** No appreciable difference in outcome between steaming and confiting.

### Meat Cooling (Juice Reabsorption)
*   **Problem:** Rapid cooling (ice bath immediately after braising) prevents juice reabsorption.
*   **Critical Threshold:** Juices stop reabsorbing once meat drops below ~50°C.
*   **Recommended Sequence (Bruno Goussot Method):**
    1.  Leave on counter in air: 20 minutes.
    2.  Tap water (room temp): 20 minutes.
    3.  Ice bath: Until chilled.
*   **Alternative:** Counter (20 min) -> Ice bath (acceptable marginal difference).
*   **Avoid:** Direct plunge into ice bath.

### Cheese Sauce Stability (Mornay)
*   **Problem:** Cheeses break (fat separates from protein/water matrix) or become grainy/ropy when heated.
*   **Solution:** Use emulsifying salts to create a stable "processed cheese" base before adding to Mornay.
*   **Ingredients:**
    *   *Emulsifying Salts:* Sodium citrate (~1% of cheese weight) or monosodium phosphate.
    *   *Acidifier:* Citric acid or lemon juice (to counteract alkalinity of salts and maintain pH).
*   **Process:**
    1.  Grate cheese finely.
    2.  Mix with emulsifying salt.
    3.  Add liquid/fat if needed to match ratios of melting cheeses (e.g., Gruyère, Velveeta).
    4.  Heat until a smooth curd set forms.
    5.  Incorporate into Mornay sauce.
*   **Reference:** University of Guelph dairy science resources.

### Fat Washing (Rancidity Prevention)
*   **Risk:** Residual fat can go rancid over time.
*   **Prevention:**
    *   Freeze fat-washed alcohol and strain.
    *   Add Vitamin E (tocopherol) as a fat-soluble antioxidant.
*   **Stability Factors:**
    *   Fat type matters (Cocoa butter is highly stable; lobster fat less stable than duck/bacon fat).
    *   Shelf life of the original fat affects the product.
    *   Proof of spirit may influence stability.

### Sauerkraut Flavoring
*   **Technique:** Add lactic acid powder to quick-pickled cabbage to mimic sauerkraut flavor.
*   **Trade-off:** Lacks the complex flavors of actual fermentation but provides the acidic profile.
*   **Form:** Powder preferred over liquid (easier to handle).

### Induction Cooktops
*   **Brand:** CookTech (US brand, Illinois).
*   **Issues:**
    *   Early units burned out when placed over ovens (electronics failure).
    *   Shattering under heavy impact (dropping pots/pan).
*   **Configuration:** Dual units (side-by-side or front-back) allow stacking like a range.
*   **Status:** Problems largely solved in last 4–5 years.

### Vacuum Sealer Maintenance
*   **Oil:** Commercial machines use vacuum oil for sealing.
*   **Maintenance:**
    *   *Cleaning:* Run pump to heat oil and boil out water contaminants.
    *   *Changing:* Run pump to heat oil -> loosen bolts -> move pump -> drain old oil (check for grit) -> fill with fresh oil (<1 quart).
    *   *Frequency:* Not often; only when contaminated.
*   **Source:** Industrial supply (McMaster, Grainger, MSC Direct).

# Ingredients & Functional Roles

*   **Pectanex SPL:** Enzyme for peeling (removes albedo) and clarification.
*   **Calcium Gluconate / Calcium Lactate:** Salts used in spherification (alginate + calcium = gel). Gluconate/Lactate preferred over Chloride for flavor neutrality.
*   **Sodium Citrate / Monosodium Phosphate:** Emulsifying salts; bind fat and protein in cheese to prevent breaking/graininess.
*   **Vitamin E (Tocopherol):** Fat-soluble antioxidant; prevents rancidity in fat-washed spirits.
*   **Lactic Acid:** Provides sauerkraut-like acidity; can be added directly to quick pickles.
*   **Everclear (190/195 proof):** High-proof alcohol for rapid infusion; requires sourcing outside NY or lab grade.
*   **Cocoa Butter:** Highly stable against oxidation (unlike some other fats).
*   **Polyethylene:** Common plastic wrap material; generally safe at low temps (contains ethyl vinyl acetate/polybutene but no plasticizers).
*   **Polycarbonate:** Contains BPA; avoid for cooking, acceptable for drinking vessels.
*   **PVC:** Contains plasticizers/phthalates; avoid for cooking.
*   **Nylon:** Used in cooking bags; considered inert.

# Strong Opinions & Recommendations

*   **Do NOT** use an ultrasonic homogenizer as a toothbrush or for cleaning teeth.
*   **Do NOT** attempt to use Quantro spherification kits for complex molecular mixology; they are designed for basic "tiny balls."
*   **Avoid** "Molecular Mixology" as a term; find it gross and dumb.
*   **Induction:** CookTech is a fine brand, but beware of shattering under impact and early electronic failures over ovens.
*   **Tinctures:** Rapid infusion is faster but yields different flavors; test before relying on it for specific recipes.
*   **Broth:** Do not be "provincial" about broth cubes; they are valid ingredients globally.
*   **Vacuum Oil:** Changing oil is simple (drain/refill) but requires heating the pump first.
*   **Confit:** The fat does not add flavor; steaming is a valid alternative.
*   **Plastics:** Avoid Polycarbonate (BPA) and PVC for cooking. Polyethylene and Nylon are generally safe at low temps.
*   **Cheese Sauce:** Adding emulsifying salts and acid is necessary for stability, even if it feels like a "shortcut."
*   **Cooling Meat:** Never plunge hot meat directly into ice; it ruins texture/juiciness. Follow the Goussot method (Air -> Water -> Ice).
*   **Fat Washing:** Add Vitamin E to prevent rancidity; freeze immediately after straining.
*   **Sauerkraut:** Adding lactic acid is a valid way to get the flavor without the time commitment of fermentation.
