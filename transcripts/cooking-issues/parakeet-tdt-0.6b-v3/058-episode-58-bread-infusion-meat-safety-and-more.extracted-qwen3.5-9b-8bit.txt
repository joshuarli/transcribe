# Cooking Issues Episode 58: Structured Notes

## Gelatin: Sheets vs. Powder
*   **Preference:** Dave Arnold prefers sheet gelatin for standard operations due to clarity, clean taste, and ease of use in whole-leaf form.
*   **Powdered Gelatin:** Preferred for clarification tasks because it is easier to measure in smaller quantities.
*   **Knox Brand:** Considered fairly good quality, equivalent to "gold" strength or higher.
*   **Recommendation:** If a recipe calls for gelatin, try sheet gelatin.
*   **Trade-off:** Leaf gelatin requires cutting up; powdered is easier for hydro recipes.

## Bagel Cocktail Infusion
*   **Base Spirit:**
    *   **Vodka:** Recommended as the first test due to high proof and neutral flavor profile. Allows for easy flavor transfer without overpowering the bagel.
    *   **Scotch:** Discouraged; the oak flavor is too intense and will overpower the bagel.
    *   **Malt Liquor (e.g., Colt 45):** Discouraged; alcohol content is too low for effective infusion.
    *   **Grappa:** Suggested as a potential alternative.
*   **Bagel Preparation:**
    *   **Slicing:** Slice thinly.
    *   **Toasting:** Toasting transfers flavor better but results in a "toasted bagel" flavor rather than raw.
    *   **Dehydration:** If avoiding toasted/brown flavors, dehydrate the bagel first to remove water. This accelerates flavor transfer into the alcohol.
    *   **Flavor Pairings:** Suggests adding tomato water or other savory flavors (e.g., tomato, onion salt, poppy seeds, sesame) to complement the bagel.
*   **Method:**
    1.  Place sliced (and optionally toasted/dehydrated) bagel in a mason jar.
    2.  Add spirit (start with high-proof vodka).
    3.  Infuse for a few days (not a month).
    4.  Decant and squeeze out the bread to recover excess liquid.
*   **Anecdote:** Dave mentions a failed "burrito cocktail" experiment involving a Chipotle burrito, beer, and centrifugation, which tasted like a Subway Italian BMT sandwich.

## Dry Cured Meats & E. Coli Safety
*   **Risk:** Dry curing (salt, dehydration, fermentation) does **not** constitute an ultimate kill step for *E. coli* (specifically O157:H7).
*   **Evidence:** Outbreaks have occurred in dry fermented salami and meat jerky (especially ground meat) where bacteria survived the process.
*   **Whole Muscle vs. Ground:**
    *   **Ground Meat:** High risk; requires a heat step.
    *   **Whole Muscle (e.g., Prosciutto):** Lower risk if the meat is not needled or decarded, as the interior is relatively sterile. High salt on the exterior may wipe out surface bacteria.
*   **US Government Recommendation:** Heat to **145°F** for 4–10 minutes (or 8 minutes) for beef-containing sausages.
*   **Workaround:** Remove beef from mixes (e.g., pepperoni) to avoid the heat requirement, as pork/veal/chicken mixes do not carry the same *E. coli* risk profile.
*   **Resources:** National Center for Home Food Preservation (literature review series on smoking and curing); FSIS (Food Safety and Inspection Service) processing procedures.
*   **Nitrates/Nitrites:** Added to control botulism and provide cured color/flavor, but do not replace the need for understanding *E. coli* risks.

## Bluefish Quality & Spinal Cord Destruction (Ikime)
*   **Technique:** Spinal cord destruction (Ikime).
    *   **Action:** Cut through the spinal column and tail to destroy the spinal cord.
    *   **Effect:** Stops electrical signals to muscles, delaying rigor mortis. Results in firmer, flakier flesh that is less mushy.
*   **Test Results (Andy from Chicago):**
    *   **Control (No Ikime):** Filleted 2–4 hours post-catch, pan-fried. Result: Mushy texture.
    *   **Ikime (Spinal Cord Destroyed):** Rested 24 hours, then pan-fried. Result: Firm, flaky, meaty, delicious.
    *   **Ikime (No Coating):** Cooked in butter, salt, pepper. Result: Market improvement over previous nights; firm and flaky.
*   **Fillet Timing:** Filleting before rigor sets in may result in firmer fillets with less gaping, as the bones hold the meat in place.
*   **Bloodline:** The dark flesh near the skin (bloodline) remains present. It can be cut out before cooking.
*   **Opinion:** Bluefish is a fantastic, abundant fish. Spinal cord destruction offers a significant opportunity to improve quality.

## Snails: Preparation and Cooking
*   **Canned Snails:** Already cooked at high temperatures (~120°C for ~70 minutes) in water/salt/court bouillon. No further cooking required.
*   **Live Snails (European Style):**
    *   **Sourcing:** Available in Europe (e.g., farm *La Ciocula*); occasionally in NY fish markets.
    *   **Purging:** Starve snails for 3–7 days in a mesh cage with no water. They seal themselves (hibernation mode) to survive transport.
    *   **Cleaning:** Wash with vinegar/water. Boil for 3 minutes in salted water to kill. Remove the "trap" (guts/black foot) – though Dave notes this step is not strictly necessary.
    *   **Cooking:** Simmer in court bouillon until tender. Serve in shells with butter and herbs (parsley or mint).
    *   **Feeding:** Feeding herbs (e.g., rosemary) to live snails does not work; they do not eat the herbs and may die.
*   **Low-Temperature Cooking:**
    *   No specific recipes found for snails.
    *   **Octopus Reference:** 5 hours at **77°C**.
    *   **Cuttlefish Reference (Keller):** 64°C for 10 hours.
    *   **Geoduck Reference (Nathan Myhrvold):** Warm through in a CVAP oven (specific temp/time unknown).
    *   **Proposed Method:** Kill step (boil/vinegar rinse) -> Low temp cook.
*   **Mucus:** Snails produce significant mucus. Must rinse with water, salt, and vinegar after the kill step before working with them.

## Crispy Pickles: Enzyme Control
*   **Problem:** Enzymes (pectinase, cellulase, etc.) in cucumbers break down pectin and cellulose, causing softening.
*   **Enzyme Location:** Concentrated in the **blossom end** (opposite the stem end).
*   **Solutions:**
    1.  **Remove Blossom End:** Cut off a slice (~1/16 inch) at the blossom end. Remove any flower material attached.
    2.  **Heat Treatment:** Pour hot liquid over pickles.
        *   **Target Temp:** **180°F** (or just below).
        *   **Goal:** Inactivate enzymes without breaking down pectin (which happens above **85°C / ~185°F**).
        *   **Note:** High heat softens the cucumber; precise control is needed.
    3.  **High Salt Levels:** Stop enzymatic breakdown and bacterial growth. Causes slight wilting via osmosis but maintains crispness.
    4.  **Pickling Lime (Calcium Hydroxide):**
        *   **Mechanism:** Calcium strengthens pectin in cell walls.
        *   **Method:** Soak vegetables in lime solution (salt + lime) for hours/overnight.
        *   **Rinse:** Soak in clean water baths afterward to remove excess calcium (over-liming creates a bizarre texture).
        *   **Effect:** Keeps vegetables firm even after cooking.
    5.  **Enzymatic Strengthening:** Use **Pectin Methyl Esterase** (commercial brand: **NovoShape** from Novozymes). Soak fruit/veg, then boil; remains firm.
*   **Variety:** Use pickling cucumbers (uniform, dense, no air voids) and pickle immediately after harvest.

## Humane Pig Slaughter (Sodium Nitrite)
*   **Concept:** Induce methemoglobinemia in wild pigs using microencapsulated sodium nitrite baits.
*   **Mechanism:** Pigs have low levels of methemoglobin reductase. Sodium nitrite converts hemoglobin to methemoglobin, preventing oxygen transport.
*   **Effect:** Pigs become woozy, pass out, and die within 1 hour without feeling stress (pigs sense excess CO2, not lack of oxygen).
*   **Comparison:** More humane than rat poison (which causes internal bleeding).
*   **Potential Application:** Feasible for humane slaughter of market pigs using food-grade sodium nitrite as an anesthetic.
*   **Research Status:** Currently used for wild hog control; potential for domestic use requires further study.
