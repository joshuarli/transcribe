# Cooking Issues: Structured Culinary Notes

## Low-Temperature Cooking & Sous Vide

### Duck Breast
*   **Temperature:** 57°C to 58°C (135°F–136°F).
    *   *Note:* 57°C may result in a slightly tough texture; 58°C is recommended to reduce "squashiness." Do not exceed 59°C.
*   **Time:** 45 minutes to 1 hour.
    *   *Warning:* Cooking longer than 1 hour can make the meat mushy or livery (especially Muscovy duck).
*   **Technique:**
    1.  **Preparation:** Squish the breast very flat against a table, skin-side down, to ensure proper contact with the pan later. Do not leave it willy-nilly in the bag.
    2.  **Cooking:** Cook in a circulator.
    3.  **Rendering:** Render the fat **after** cooking.
    4.  **Searing:** Sear traditionally in a pan after cooking to crisp the skin.
    *   *Alternative:* Some modernist chefs sear before and after using "cryo searing" (dry ice bag) to freeze the surface solid and prevent overcooking.
*   **Equipment:** Sous vide professional circulator.

### Pork Shoulder (Rose)
*   **Temperature:** 60°C to 62°C (140°F–144°F).
    *   *Note:* At 60°C, the meat remains quite pink and soft. Go up to 62°C if visual pinkness is undesirable. Do not exceed 62°C.
*   **Time:** 36 to 48 hours.
*   **Technique:**
    *   Brown the shoulder first.
    *   Cook in a circulator.
    *   Apply a hard sear at the end.
*   **Muscle Identification:**
    *   Pork shoulder contains multiple muscles. Muscles with high connective tissue benefit from long, low-temperature cooking (similar to short ribs).
    *   Muscles with less connective tissue may appear fibrous if not cooked long enough, but will be tender and not dry.
    *   *Visual Cue:* Look for marbling and connective tissue distribution. On pork belly, most muscles are good, except one oval-shaped muscle on top running opposite the grain, which is naturally tender and does not require long cooking.

### Foie Gras Terrine/Torchon
*   **Temperature:** Low to mid 50s°C (approx. 55°C–57°C).
    *   *Range:* 60°C is mentioned as an upper limit for higher temp cooks, but lower is preferred to minimize fat leakage.
*   **Time:** Short duration (exact time not specified, but emphasized as "not very long").
*   **Technique:**
    *   Pack in a terrine mold inside a bag or just inside a bag.
    *   Maintain a slight delta T (water bath temp slightly higher than target internal temp) to cook quickly and prevent excessive fat leakage.
*   **Reference:** Recipes from Bruno Goussault, Georges Pralus, and Juan Roca.

### Pheasant
*   **Whole Roast:** Not recommended (legs tough, breast overcooks easily).
*   **Recommended Method:**
    1.  Separate breasts from legs.
    2.  **Legs:** Braise or cook separately (tougher). Use bones for stock.
    3.  **Breasts:**
        *   **Temperature:** 57°C to 58°C.
        *   **Time:** ~20 minutes (due to thinness).
        *   **Process:** Cook, cool down, then quick-sear very high heat to crisp skin.
*   **Warning:** Do not cook whole pheasant for 1.5 hours; it is too long.

### Root Beer Foam Stabilization
*   **Problem:** Small head that dies off quickly.
*   **Solution:** Add hydrocolloids to stabilize foam.
*   **Ingredients:**
    *   **PGA (Propylene Glycol Alginate):** Modified alginate (seaweed gel). Not 100% natural. Stable in acidic environments.
    *   **Dosage:** ~80 parts per million (ppm).
    *   **Preparation:** Dissolve in water, add a small amount of solution to the product before final fermentation.
*   **Alternative:** Beer foam stabilizers (likely pectin-based), potentially cheaper or more natural than PGA.
*   **Theory:** Head stability relates to surface-active principles of the liquid; ingredients left over from barley in beer contribute to head retention.

### General Sous Vide Parameters
*   **Starting Temperature Assumption:** Most recipes assume food starts straight from the refrigerator (~38°F–40°F / ~3°C–4°C).
*   **Time Buffer:** Build in 10%–15% overage on cooking time to account for variance in starting temperatures.
*   **Precision:** Critical for "delta temperature" cooking (e.g., cooking fish to 55°C–56°C in a 60°C bath) where seconds matter. Less critical for thicker cuts with built-in time buffers.

## Ingredients & Functional Properties

### Whole Wheat Pasta
*   **Issues:**
    *   **Texture:** Bran interferes with the gluten matrix, resulting in poor texture.
    *   **Flavor:** Often tastes like raw field corn; prone to rancidity due to fat content if stored long-term.
    *   **Perception:** "Healthier" whole wheat products (e.g., Barilla Plus) are often culinarily unsatisfying.
*   **Improvement (DIY):**
    *   Use very finely ground whole wheat flour (e.g., Shapati flour).
    *   Add **Vital Wheat Gluten** to increase snap.
    *   Add **Consejo** (alkaline powder) or **Sodium Carbonate** (not baking soda) to strengthen the gluten network and add alkalinity.
    *   Must be extraordinarily fresh.

### Lulo
*   **Properties:** Creates a creamy head when blended into a juice/shaken drink.
*   **Usage:** Essential fruit in Colombia; makes an aesthetic drink.

### Other Tropical Fruits (Colombia)
*   **Uchuva:** Gooseberry-like; excellent; often free with mango steams.
*   **Guanabana:** Favorite fruit; available in great quantities.
*   **Passion Fruit:** Many variants. **Grenadilla** is the most favorite; **Cuba** is smaller but delicious.
*   **Tomate de Árbol:** Bitter skin, interesting flavor.
*   **Pataya:** Dragon fruit (delicious, unlike the watery versions often found elsewhere).
*   **Mango Steams:** Delicious, though not necessarily from the region.

### Hydrocolloids
*   **Sources:** Modernist Pantry, Terra Spice, Amazon, homebrew shops.
*   **Note:** No great homebrew shops or specialty markets for hydrocolloids found in NYC (except a small one at Whole Foods).

### Potato Toxins
*   **Compounds:** Solanine and tomatine (defensive compounds against fungi/bacteria/humans).
*   **Green Potatoes:** Chlorophyll presence indicates increased solanine levels.
*   **Preparation:** Peel green potatoes thoroughly.
*   **Traditional Method:** Dipping wild potatoes in **Kaolin** (clay) to adsorb toxins (mimics animal digestion). Kaolin is also used as a wine fining agent.

### Chunio Blanco
*   **Description:** Inca freeze-dried potato (frozen, thrown in a well, frozen/thawed again).
*   **Flavor Profile:** Smells and tastes like goat's bedding.
*   **Culinary Challenge:** Dave offers a heritage meat prize to anyone who can prepare a decent dish with these potatoes.

## Caller Q&A

### Matthew (Cartagena & Duck)
*   **Q1:** Food recommendations for Cartagena, Colombia? Specifically fruit, seafood, or ingredients to bring home?
    *   **A:**
        *   **Restaurant:** Vera (Hotel Asia). Cool indoor/outdoor design with a waterfall.
        *   **Fruit:** Lulo (make a drink/juice), Uchuva (gooseberry-like, free with mango), Guanabana (favorite), Passion fruit (Grenadilla and Cuba), Tomate de Árbol, Pataya (dragon fruit).
        *   **Warning:** Avoid dive bars; locals can be aggressive.
*   **Q2:** Low temp duck breast (Muscovy) at 58°C for 1.5 hours resulted in chewy meat. Should I cook longer? Should I render fat before or after?
    *   **A:**
        *   **Rendering:** Always render **after** cooking.
        *   **Searing:** Sear after cooking. Some do before/after with cryo-searing.
        *   **Time:** 1.5 hours is likely too long, making it mushy or livery. Try 45 minutes to 1 hour at 57°C–58°C.
        *   **Temp Adjustment:** If tough at 57°C, try 58°C. Do not go higher than 59°C.

### Joshua Stokes (Foie Gras Terrine)

    *   **A:**

### Andrew (Tongue Brushing)
*   **Q:** Does brushing/scraping the tongue damage taste buds and affect the palate?
    *   **A:**
        *   **Damage:** Yes, abrasion damages taste cells.
        *   **Recovery:** Taste cells regenerate every ~10 days. No permanent loss.
        *   **Palate Impact:** Bad breath/halitosis affects the palate more than losing a few taste buds.
        *   **Professional Advice:** Do not brush with toothpaste before tasting/cooking. It wipes out the palate. If brushing is necessary, chew carrots or drink fluids to neutralize the "toothbrush taste."

        *   **Legs:** Braise or cook separately. Use bones for stock.

### Sebastian (Root Beer Head)
*   **Q:** How to get a long-lasting, foamy head in homemade root beer?
    *   **A:**
        *   **Additive:** Propylene Glycol Alginate (PGA).
        *   **Dosage:** ~80 ppm.
        *   **Method:** Dissolve in water, add to product before final fermentation.
        *   **Note:** PGA is not 100% natural but is stable in acid. Alternatives exist (pectin-based) but PGA is the pro standard.

### Mark Younger (Starting Temps)
*   **Q:** Do sous vide times assume food starts from the fridge, or is there a standard starting temp?
    *   **A:**
        *   **Assumption:** Most recipes assume straight from the fridge (~38°F–40°F).
        *   **Reasoning:** Convenience and consistency.
        *   **Adjustment:** Build in 10%–15% time overage to account for temp variance.
        *   **Critical Cases:** For "delta temperature" cooking (thin fish, razor margins), starting temp is vital. Use programs like SousVide.com to calculate time based on starting temp.
