# Reverse Spherification Troubleshooting

## Caller Issue: Rum Sphere Failure
*   **Problem:** Spheres dispersed on the surface ("pancake effect") instead of dropping; bath was thick but clear.
*   **Root Causes Identified:**
    *   **Alginate Concentration Too Low:** Caller used 2.5g per 500g water (0.4% or 4g/L). This is insufficient for rapid gelation.
    *   **Lack of Density/Heft:** Rum is light; without added viscosity, it floats and spreads rather than sinking to form a drop.
    *   **Air Bubbles:** Cloudy rum indicates trapped air, which prevents proper drop formation.
    *   **Calcium Concentration:** Caller used 30g/L calcium lactate. Dave notes this is high (3%) and suggests dialing back once gelling is achieved.

## Recommended Parameters & Techniques
*   **Alginate Bath Concentration:**
    *   Increase to **15g/L** (Dave's recommendation for robust gelation).
    *   Acceptable range for high viscosity alginate: **7g/L to 8g/L**.
    *   *Note:* 4g/L is too low for quick formation.
*   **Alginate Type:**
    *   Use **High Viscosity Alginate** (e.g., FMC Biopolymer).
    *   Medium viscosity is also an option but requires higher concentration.
*   **Bath Preparation:**
    *   **Stabilizer:** Sodium Citrate acts as a pH buffer (prevents alginate from gelling prematurely if pH drops) and sequestrant (binds excess calcium).
        *   *Alternative Sequestrant:* Sodium Hexametaphosphate (stronger).
    *   **Temperature:** Heat the alginate bath to decrease viscosity and help release air bubbles. Do not boil alcohol out of the flavor base.
*   **Flavor Base Modifications:**
    *   **Add Body:** Add glucose syrup, corn syrup, or invert sugar to increase density so the base sinks.
    *   **Remove Air:**
        *   Let cloudy mixtures settle.
        *   Use a vacuum machine (if available).
        *   **Alternative Method:** If a vacuum is unavailable, use a **Silpat**. Place drops on the Silpat, freeze them, then thaw instantly in the hot alginate bath. This forces the drop underneath the surface.
*   **Post-Formation Handling:**
    *   **Separation:** If spheres touch in the bath, they bond via alginate. Immediately rinse in **pure water** to separate.
    *   **Storage:**
        *   Store in the **flavor base** (with calcium) to last indefinitely.
        *   Store in a sugar/alcohol solution matching the ball's sweetness and alcohol level (intermediate option).
        *   *Avoid:* Storing in pure water (leaches flavor/color quickly).

## Equipment & Ingredients
*   **Alginate Brands:** FMC Biopolymer (High/Medium viscosity). Modernist Pantry (brand unknown to Dave regarding viscosity specs).
*   **Calcium Sources:** Calcium Lactate or Calcium Lactate Gluconate (Gluconate makes a softer gel).
*   **Thickeners:** Xanthan gum (ensure no air bubbles), Glucose Syrup, Corn Syrup.

# Starches: Ultra-Tex vs. Ultra-Disperse

## Product Comparison
*   **Ultra-Tex (National Starch):**
    *   **Type:** Precooked, prehydrated starch.
    *   **Usage:** Requires **high shear mixing** (blender, food processor) to disperse.
    *   **Risk:** Without high shear, it gels into "pearls" or globules immediately upon contact with liquid (similar to adding starch to hot gravy without a slurry).
*   **Ultra-Disperse (National Starch):**
    *   **Type:** Agglomerated, precooked, prehydrated starch.
    *   **Appearance:** Grainy texture; does not clump into balls.
    *   **Usage:** Can be stirred directly into liquids; requires less high shear.
    *   **Tradeoff:** More expensive than Ultra-Tex, but recommended for home/high-end chefs due to ease of use.

# Rotary Evaporator (Rotovap) Maintenance

## Cleaning Protocol
*   **Contaminants:** Inorganic poisons, organic poisons, biohazards.
*   **Cleaning Steps:**
    1.  Soak in **bleach** (for biohazards).
    2.  Use **ultrasonic cleaners**.
    3.  Multiple changes of water.
    4.  Use detergents that attack both polar and non-polar substances.
    5.  Repeat process.
*   **Specific Solvent Decontamination:**
    *   Research specific decontamination procedures for nasty solvents (e.g., Carbon Tetrachloride).
    *   Consider heavy-duty cleaners like brewery wash (powdered) for chemical deposits.
*   **Condenser Types:**
    *   **Regular Coil Condenser:** Difficult to clean; impossible to reach nooks and crannies with hands.
    *   **Cold Finger Condenser:** Disassemblable; allows hand access for thorough cleaning.
    *   **Best Option:** Cold finger condenser with a coiled insert sleeve (combines benefits of both).
*   **Coolants:**
    *   Cold finger typically uses Dry Ice/Acetone or Dry Ice/Ethanol.
    *   Dave prefers **Liquid Nitrogen** (easier to source, though storage is an issue).
    *   *Note:* Cold finger has lower surface area than coil condensers unless using super-cold agents.

# Ice Cream Formulation

## Substituting Sugar with Glucose Syrup
*   **Goal:** Improve texture, prevent crystallization, maintain stability.
*   **Mechanism:** Glucose syrup increases viscosity (denser mouthfeel) and depresses freezing point.
*   **Calculation Logic (Relative to Sucrose):**
    *   **Sweetness:** Glucose 28 DE is ~40% as sweet as sucrose (dry weight basis).
    *   **Freezing Point Depression:** Glucose 28 DE provides ~50% of the freezing point depression of sucrose.
    *   **Adjustment:** To match sweetness, you must add more solids.
        *   *Example:* Removing 200g sugar requires adding ~400g of solids from Glucose 28 DE to match sweetness, resulting in a denser mix.
*   **Recipe Balancing:**
    *   Calculate relative sweetness and relative freezing point depression against sucrose (baseline 1.0).
    *   Target sucrose levels: 13-18% solids for standard texture.
    *   If reducing sweetness without drastically changing freezing point: Add glucose syrup + small amounts of alcohol.
*   **Additives:** Gelatin helps inhibit crystallization alongside hydrocolloids.

# Sherry Vinegar Production

## Process Parameters
*   **Alcohol Content:**
    *   Sherry is fortified (high ABV).
    *   **Target:** Dilute sherry to **7% - 9% alcohol** (preferably starting lower at 7%) before inoculation.
    *   *Reason:* High alcohol inhibits *Acetobacter* growth.
*   **Sulfur Dioxide:** High levels in wine can inhibit *Acetobacter*.
*   **Method:**
    *   **Aerobic:** Requires air for *Acetobacter* growth.
    *   **Mother:** Mainly bacterial cellulose; floats on top.
    *   **Technique:**
        *   Old school: Cask with holes/screen for airflow.
        *   Fast method: Increase surface area (e.g., mix wood chips).
        *   Maintenance: Remove 3/4 of vinegar, replace with fresh liquid without disturbing the mother.
    *   **Aging:**
        *   Can age in wooden barrels (small ones rare).
        *   **Pasteurization:** Can be used to stop bacterial activity and prevent acid consumption, allowing for flavor control.
        *   **Air Exclusion:** Can exclude air to stop oxidation after acetic acid formation.
