# Enzymatic Peeling of Citrus
*   **Enzyme:** Pectinex Ultra SPL (eats pectin and hemicellulose).
*   **Source:** Available from Modernist Pantry.
*   **Ratio:** 4g of enzyme per 1L of water (Dave notes he previously used 3g but now uses 4g for reliability).
*   **Temperature:** Room temperature (warm). Do not refrigerate; cold slows the enzyme.
*   **Time:** Soak duration is flexible but room temperature accelerates the process.
*   **Equipment:** Vacuum sealer bag (optional but helpful for penetration).
*   **Preparation Steps:**
    1.  Break citrus into small segments (quarters or smaller) without cutting into the vesicles (fruit sacs).
        *   *Note:* Grapefruits are easy; pomelos are hard due to high pith content.
    2.  Keep peels as whole as possible. Place peels in the bag with the segments.
    3.  Add enzyme solution (4g/L).
    4.  Soak.
    5.  *Optional:* Use a vacuum sealer to force solution into the pith faster.
    6.  *Optional:* Use ice water to facilitate vacuum sealing, then transfer to a warm water bath to melt ice and activate enzyme cooking.
    7.  After soaking, use a toothbrush to scrub away the melted albedo (white pith).
*   **Limitations:** The enzyme will not penetrate intact membranes between segments if the fruit is left whole in a bag. Segments must be separated.

# Vegan Whipped Desserts (Soy Base)
*   **Problem:** Soy milk lacks the fat content and structure to whip and hold texture like dairy cream. Xanthan gum creates a "snot" texture; LBG fails similarly.
*   **Solution Strategy:** Mimic commercial "ready whip" products (e.g., SoyaToo).
*   **Key Ingredients:**
    *   **Fat:** Solid fat is critical for body. Use coconut oil or palm kernel oil (fractionated).
    *   **Stabilizer/Structure:** Carrageenan (specifically **Genatine** by CP Kelco, a specialist carrageenan for whipping/gelatin replacement).
    *   **Mass/Body:** Maltodextrin.
    *   **Flavor:** Tartaric acid, vanilla.
    *   **Whipping Agent:** Nitrous oxide (N2O) can be used to whip the mixture.
*   **Technique:**
    1.  Chill mixture thoroughly ("cold as hell").
    2.  Add solid fat first to increase whipping quality.
    3.  Add maltodextrin and Genatine.
    4.  Whip.

# Steak Seasoning Techniques
*   **Pre-salting (1+ hour in advance):**
    *   *Dave's Opinion:* Not worth it for immediate service (within a couple of hours).
    *   *Reasoning:* While it seasons through and retains moisture, the textural breakdown and color change from over-salting beforehand outweigh the minor flavor advantages.
    *   *Exception:* Long-cooked dishes (braises, pot roasts) where seasoning must penetrate fully.
*   **Recommended Method for Steaks:**
    1.  Salt the exterior of the steak immediately before searing (generous amount).
    2.  Sear aggressively.
    3.  Slice the steak into pieces before serving.
    4.  Salt the cut faces of the slices if additional seasoning is needed.
*   **Comparison:** Unlike bread (where pre-salting is critical) or long braises, steaks do not require pre-salting for optimal texture if served quickly.

# Stock Safety (Ruhlman vs. McGee)
*   **Context:** Ruhlman suggests leaving stock on the stove unrefrigerated for days, boiling it up when needed (historical practice). Harold McGee advises against this.
*   **Dave's Stance:** Agrees with McGee.
*   **Reasoning:**
    *   **Safety:** Not 100% safe. Bacteria can grow if not brought to a proper boil immediately.
    *   **Quality:** Significant quality degradation occurs as stock sits at room temperature over time.
    *   **Recommendation:** Refrigerate stock.
*   **Cured Meat Exception:**
    *   Properly cured meat (sufficient salt/nitrites) is resistant to bacterial growth.
    *   *Caveat:* "Cured" does not mean "immune to contamination." External contaminants can grow on cured meat.
    *   *Risk:* If salt/nitrite levels are insufficient or pH is wrong, serious issues (e.g., botulism) can occur.
    *   *Verdict:* If properly cured and handled, nothing bad will grow, but one should still take it with a "grain of salt" regarding external contamination.

# Substitutes for Juniper Berries
*   **Substitute:** Eastern Red Cedar berries (*Juniperus virginiana*).
*   **Availability:** Common in the US; often used ornamentally.
*   **Edibility:** Edible; used historically by Native American tribes (leaves, berries, bark).
*   **Flavor Profile:**
    *   Less powerful aromatic quality than traditional *Juniperus communis*.
    *   Not as "baller" (intense) a juniper flavor.
*   **Usage:** Good for applications where a milder, distinct cedar flavor is acceptable.
*   **Safety Warning:**
    *   Do not eat large quantities ("boatloads").
    *   Some juniper species are toxic; ensure correct identification.
    *   Mild toxicity possible; consult herbal references.

# Knife Recommendations: Shun x Michel Bras
*   **Model:** Shun x Michel Bras line.
*   **Coating:** Titanium oxide (not platinum).
    *   *Properties:* Very hard (used on high-speed drill bits), prevents rust.
    *   *Durability:* Does not scratch easily.
*   **Blade Construction:**
    *   Two grades of stainless steel: Softer outer layer (toughness), harder center (edge retention).
    *   Titanium metal core (tough, non-rusting).
*   **Geometry:**
    *   50-degree total bevel (15 degrees per side).
    *   Symmetrical sharpening (like traditional French/German knives), unlike many Japanese knives.
    *   Easy to sharpen for European users.
*   **Performance:**
    *   Balanced well.
    *   *Dave's Opinion:* Will not cut appreciably better than less expensive Shun/Kershaw knives due to similar blade chemistry.
    *   *Value:* High price tag for marginal cutting performance gain; primarily aesthetic and ergonomic benefits.
*   **Philosophy on Sharpness:**
    *   "Knives are like puppies": Best when new, problems arise later.
    *   *Recommendation:* Prefer softer steel that sharpens easily over super-hard, difficult-to-sharpen steel.
    *   *Strategy:* Sharpen a lesser-quality blade right before use rather than letting a high-quality blade dull for months.

# Cauliflower Browning (Brassica Chemistry)
*   **Observation:** Cauliflower florets turned brown/caramelized during slow simmering in half-and-half, despite tasting bad.
*   **Cause:** Sulfur-containing compounds inherent to brassicas (cauliflower, cabbage, broccoli, Brussels sprouts).
*   **Chemistry:**
    *   Prolonged cooking causes sulfur compounds to react.
    *   Results in off-odors ("stinky"), lipid oxidation, and color changes (browning).
*   **Note:** Dave mentions Harold McGee would know the specific chemical mechanism immediately.
