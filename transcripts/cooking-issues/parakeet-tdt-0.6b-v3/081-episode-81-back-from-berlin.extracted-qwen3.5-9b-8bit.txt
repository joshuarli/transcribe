# Wild Game Brining and Color Issues
*   **Problem:** Brining wild game (reindeer, lagopus/grouse) with 4–10% sodium chloride solution causes a gray line (2–3 mm) around the meat when cooked low temperature. The line is more visible on darker meat.
*   **Cause:** Salting increases the rate of metmyoglobin (brown) production and accelerates myoglobin denaturation. This effect is magnified at lower cooking temperatures.
*   **Myoglobin States:**
    *   *Deoxymyoglobin:* Purple/brown (vacuum bagged, low oxygen). Denatures slower under heat than other forms.
    *   *Oxymyoglobin:* Bright pink/red (fresh meat).
    *   *Metmyoglobin:* Brown (cooked look).
*   **Solutions Tested (Ineffective):** Ascorbic acid, citric acid, antioxidants.
*   **Other Additives Tested (Ineffective):** Fat, polyphosphates, soy proteins, casein. None affected the rate of myoglobin denaturation.
*   **Recommendation:** Do not brine if doing low-temperature cooking unless flavor is the primary goal (texture changes are also a risk). If flavor is needed, accept the color line as unavoidable if salt penetrates the meat. Acid marinades also accelerate denaturation.

# Rye Tortilla Preparation
*   **Technique:** Grinding whole rye berries at home is difficult; standard mortars and pestles or the "Corona" grinder do not work well.
*   **Method:**
    1.  Blend whole rye berries with enough water to create a puree.
    2.  Add *maseca* (corn flour) to the puree to thicken it back up to a workable dough consistency.
*   **Result:** Produces tortillas with strong rye flavor and great texture, suitable for frying to a crunch.
*   **Note:** *Maseca* is a corn flour product; while previously criticized, it is effective for this specific thickening application.

# Yeast in Distillation
*   **Function:** Yeast strains are the primary source of flavor in distilled beverages (esters, fusel oils, congeners), aside from the raw material aroma.
*   **Experimentation:** Using different yeasts (e.g., German Hefe Weizen, British fruity ale yeast) at high fermentation temperatures creates wildly different distillates (some full of acetone, others not).
*   **Industry Practice:** Distilleries rarely advertise yeast strains, focusing instead on stills and raw materials. They must maintain consistency by propagating specific yeast strains over years rather than experimenting freely.
*   **Specific Strains Mentioned:**
    *   Dry yeast isolated from Guatemala sugar cane (used for *rhum agricole*): Phenolic and plasticky; too strong even with tight cuts.
    *   Yeast from cane fields: Used by Havana Club for consistency, though they claim it is "endogenous" to the area.

# Soda Stabilization (Particulate Matter)
*   **Problem:** Fresh juices (cucumber, watermelon, herbs) settle out overnight in kegs.
*   **Goal:** Stabilize suspension without scaring consumers with artificial-sounding labels.
*   **Recommended Agents:**
    *   **Pectin:** Best option for label declaration (can list source like "citrus peel" or "apple pomace"). Effective for particles small enough to pass through a coffee filter.
    *   **Contact:** CP Kelco (specializes in pectins and stability).
    *   **Alternative:** TIC Gums (specializes in legal label declarations for gums).
    *   **Avoid:** Propylene glycol alginate (PGA) or gum arabic/xanthan mixes. These add too much body, causing massive foam issues in carbonated drinks.
*   **Alternative Approach:** Rack the soda (wine method) to remove particles, then increase CO2 volume to compensate for lost body.

# VersaWhip (Whipping Agent)
*   **Composition:** Modified soy protein or whey protein (two different versions exist).
*   **Function:** Whipping agent and foam stabilizer. Large, charged molecules aid aeration.
*   **Usage Tip:** Unlike egg whites, VersaWhip may sit for a while before suddenly whipping up ("nothing, nothing... bang"). Do not give up immediately.
*   **Application:** Replacement for egg whites and gelatin in meringues, mousses, and hot/cold foams.
*   **Cost:** Approx. $5 per package (Modernist Pantry promo code CI81).

# Water Filtration for Coffee
*   **Problem:** Reverse Osmosis (RO) filters remove almost everything, resulting in water that tastes bad for coffee. Coffee requires mineralization to taste good.
*   **Solution:**
    *   Do not use standalone RO systems.
    *   Use a multi-stage filter system: A large, somewhat porous first filter to capture debris/cysts, followed by finer filters.
    *   **Pressure Warning:** Multiple filters decrease water pressure; ensure the system maintains adequate pressure.
    *   **Brand Mention:** GE home water systems.
    *   **Advanced Option:** RO followed by a remineralization kit.
*   **Context:** Water in Moscow may be too mineralized or have specific taste issues; standard multi-stage filtration may not be sufficient without specific parameters.

# Gum Syrup
*   **Composition:** Sugar, water, and a "boatload" of gum arabic.
*   **Properties of Gum Arabic:**
    *   Highly branched hydrocolloid polymer (similar to methylcellulose).
    *   Large molecular weight but not overly thick even in heavy solutions.
    *   Contains protein that acts as an emulsifier.
    *   Maintains emulsifying capabilities even when radically diluted.
*   **Benefits:**
    *   Stabilizes soda syrups containing oils.
    *   Adds body to drinks.
    *   Makes bubbles hold longer in shaken drinks.
    *   Creates a creamy mouthfeel and better foam head.
*   **Recipe Note:** Do not skimp on the gum arabic.
*   **Modern Variation:** "Tick simple" (mixture of gum arabic and Xanthan gum) used for emulsified fat drinks (e.g., cold buttered rum, nutty orjays).

# Foie Gras Ban (California)
*   **Issue:** Force-feeding (*gavage*) inflates animal livers.
*   **Arguments:**
    *   *Against Ban:* *Gavage* is an ancient practice (Romans); some producers treat animals humanely (e.g., Hudson Valley/Mamafuku, Thomas Keller's suppliers). Animals may not fear the procedure.
    *   *For Ban:* Activists target an easy win to gain momentum against larger industrial farming issues.
*   **Dave Arnold's Stance:**
    *   Believes in humane methods for all farming.
    *   Opposes banning a product chefs want to serve if produced humanely.
    *   Notes that banning foie gras allows horrible factory farming practices to continue unchecked while targeting a specific, expensive product to make consumers feel guilty.
    *   Personal view: Could live without it; not a favorite food.

# Low-Temperature Sausage Cooking
*   **Technique:** Cooking sausages (e.g., beer brats) at low temperatures.
*   **Casing Handling:** **Do not pierce the casing.**
    *   Water-based liquids (beer) penetrate the membrane, leaching flavors out and adding unwanted flavors in.
    *   Oil-based liquids (ziplock bag) are acceptable.
    *   Piercing causes loss of juices upon cooking.

# Sous Vide Chicken Breast to Fried Chicken
*   **Sous Vide Parameters:** 56°C for 2.5 to 3 hours (results in anemic-looking meat).
*   **Frying Challenge:** Need a batter/coating that adheres well, carries herbs/spices, and fries quickly without soaking the meat.
*   **Recommended Batter:**
    *   **Base:** Buttermilk, egg, baking soda, baking powder.
    *   **Flavor:** Add all desired flavors to the liquid base.
    *   **Coating:** Straight flour.
    *   **Process:** Pull chicken from bath, flash dry to form a pellicle, coat in flour, then fry.
    *   **Note:** This differs from traditional Southern techniques but works well for low-temp cooked meat.

# Capsaicin Reduction in Habaneros
*   **Proposed Method:** Emulsify trimmed pepper flesh with an oleophilic (fat-loving) medium (e.g., olive oil, casein), centrifuge, and filter to separate capsaicin.
*   **Assessment:** Unlikely to be effective.
    *   Capsaicin is an oleoresin, but centrifugation may not extract enough to make a significant dent.
    *   Research on trichloro-centrifugally separated capsaicin at lower speeds is lacking.
    *   No high hopes for this specific technique.
