# Potato Peeling Techniques & Equipment
*   **Y-Peeler (Recommended):**
    *   **Function:** Significantly faster than straight peelers for volumes under 20–30 lbs.
    *   **Mechanism:** Uses a "slap shot" motion; user holds potato in palm, rotates with thumb/index while peeler moves.
    *   **Trade-offs:** Requires learning curve; users may feel they will nick their thumb initially.
    *   **Recommendation:** Avoid serrated edge peelers (poor performance).
    *   **Specific Product:** Coon Recon Corporation Y-peelers (plastic, ~$4, available in colors).
*   **Blanching Method (Rub-off):**
    *   **Process:** Blanch 15 minutes, dunk in ice water, rub skin off.
    *   **Function:** Kills surface enzymes to prevent discoloration if potatoes are not immediately submerged in water.
    *   **Limitation:** Does not remove black spots inside the potato; a knife or peeler is still required for those spots.
*   **Industrial Methods:**
    *   **Abrasive Drum Peeler:** Drum with sandpaper inside spins; potatoes tumble in water to create peel sludge. Common in Europe and factories.
    *   **High-Pressure Water Jet:** Blasts out dark areas on potatoes.

# Equipment: Cast Iron & Woks
*   **Seasoning Process:**
    *   **Mechanism:** Thin layer of oil/fat heated to polymerize, forming a solid network (similar to drying oil in paint) or protective iron oxide (magnetite).
    *   **Technique:** Apply thin layers (thick gloppy layers flake off). Heat in oven above frying temp (~450°F). Turn upside down to let excess oil drip off before polymerizing.
    *   **Surface Prep:** Sanded/polished surfaces (bare metal) season better than rough sandpapery surfaces.
    *   **Scallion/Garlic Chive Method:**
        *   **Function:** Rubbing chives/scallions on the pan while heating moves oil evenly and removes bad flavors/free iron from the surface.
        *   **Why Chives:** Do not scorch badly; can be charred to reach the "black" seasoning point.
        *   **Why Not Garlic:** Creates scorched, awful flavor.
*   **Wok Types:**
    *   **Hand-Hammered:** Sheet steel hammered into shape.
    *   **Spun:** Disc pressed between wooden chucks and spun.
    *   **Stamped:** Hydraulic press.
    *   **Note:** No confirmed difference in porosity or cooking capability found.
*   **Utensils:** Metal spatulas are safe and helpful for knocking stuck bits off well-seasoned woks.

# Equipment: Oven Hacks (Pizza Crust)
*   **Aluminum Slab Method:**
    *   **Theory:** Deliver instantaneous heat "wallop" to the crust bottom.
    *   **Properties:** Aluminum is a super-fast conductor (near copper) but low mass (stores less heat than stone).
    *   **Advantage:** Heats and cools quickly; oven replenishes heat faster than with a stone, preventing lag during multiple pizzas.
    *   **Thickness:** ~1/4 inch to 3/4 inch.
    *   **Cost:** ~1/2 inch starts getting expensive due to weight pricing jumps.
    *   **Sourcing:** Aluminum supply stores or McMaster-Carr.
*   **Stainless Steel Slab:**
    *   **Verdict:** Terrible. Slow heat conductor, heavy, expensive.
*   **Seasoning Aluminum:**
    *   **Verdict:** Do not season. Forms its own oxide; cannot build a polymerized oil film.

# Ingredients & Functional Properties
*   **Heavy Cream (Whipped Cream):**
    *   **Optimal:** Non-ultra-pasteurized, high fat content.
    *   **Issue:** Ultra-pasteurized may not whip as well; low-fat creams require heavy stabilizers.
    *   **Over-whipping:** Emulsion inverts; fat agglomerates; texture becomes grainy/watery; develops buttery taste.
    *   **Stabilization:** Add gelatin mix for stiff moussses.
    *   **Flavor Tip:** Add a pinch of salt to round out flavor.
*   **Onions/Alliums:**
    *   **Interaction with Iron:** Sulfur compounds interact with iron, inhibiting corrosion but transferring bad flavors to the onion.
    *   **Knife Care:** Rubbing onions on carbon steel knives develops patina but ruins the onion's taste.
*   **Fruit Juice Concentration:**
    *   **Measurement:** Use a Brix refractometer to measure sugar content (% by weight).
    *   **Target:** Sorbet/Ice cream mix should be ~17–20% sugar.
    *   **Calculation:** Determine juice percentage in recipe + added sugar to calculate required Brix.
    *   **Equipment:** 0–32 Brix refractometers are cheap but insufficient for simple syrups (50–66 Brix). Electronic 0–85 Brix models (~$40–50) are recommended for high concentrations.

# Techniques & Anecdotes
*   **Anesthetizing Crustaceans:**
    *   **Method:** Use clove oil (available at Whole Foods) to anesthetize lobsters/crabs before cooking.
    *   **Result:** Meat tastes cleaner, sweeter; animal does not twitch/move when boiled.
    *   **Bivalve Warning:** Do not use clove oil on oysters/clams/mussels. The oil flavor permeates the juices/fluids inside the shell, ruining the taste test.
    *   **Alternative:** Stabbing through ganglia loses internal juices/flavor.
    *   **Historical Note:** Flavored oysters/clams by having them "drink" flavored stock (e.g., carrot juice + cardamom + aquarium salt) before shucking.
*   **Centrifuging Purees:**
    *   **Application:** Effective for nut oils (walnut, pistachio, hazelnut, etc.).
    *   **Inefficiency:** Very inefficient for tomato paste or bean purees.
    *   **Factors:** Success depends on water binding to puree and physical water vs. solids ratio.
    *   **Enhancement:** Enzymes can break down pectin to increase yield (may affect texture).
*   **Clarification:**
    *   **Centrifuges:** Used for high-density items like nut oils.
    *   **Tomato Paste:** Spinning yields a tiny amount of delicious juice at the top.
