# Cooking Issues: Structured Notes

## Equipment & Technology

### Hamilton Beach Turkey Roaster (Model 32503)
*   **Type:** 18-quart electric roaster (discontinued model).
*   **Construction:** Pull-out washable pan; turkey sits on a rack inside the pan.
*   **Heating Mechanism:** Plate heating (similar to a Dutch oven with its own heat source).
*   **Wattage:** Approximately 1400 watts (representative model).
*   **Deep Frying Feasibility:**
    *   **Verdict:** Technically possible but not recommended.
    *   **Pros:** Gets hot enough to fry; frees up the main oven.
    *   **Cons:**
        *   **Heat Recovery:** Low wattage relative to the large volume (18 quarts) means slow recovery when adding food. A small load might work; a whole chicken or large load will cause drastic temperature drops.
        *   **Temperature Control:** Likely inaccurate.
        *   **Scorching Risk:** Heating element is at the bottom. Particles (batter, flour) falling to the bottom will scorch against the heated plate. Good fryers float the element above the oil to prevent this.
        *   **Safety:** High risk of overflow/ignition; insurance/liability concerns.
    *   **Modification:** Can bypass the internal thermostat using a PID temperature controller and thermocouple set to "on/off" mode for better accuracy and faster recovery, though power limitations may still exist.

### Sous Vide Circulators & Water Baths
*   **Power vs. Volume:**
    *   **750 Watts:** Adequate for small vessels (3 quarts to ~12 liters / half Lexan).
    *   **1000+ Watts:** Required for full-size Lexan baths to maintain temperature under load.
    *   **Limitation:** Lower wattage circulators (e.g., 750W) cannot reliably heat or maintain temperature in large baths (full-size Lexan), especially at higher temperatures.
*   **Temperature & Heat Loss:**
    *   **55°C:** Easier to maintain due to lower atmospheric vapor loss.
    *   **85°C+:** Requires significantly more energy due to high vapor loss; 1000W+ circulators often struggle to maintain accuracy or drive temperature in large baths at these levels.
*   **Recovery Rate:** Critical for maintaining temperature when dropping large food loads into the bath.

### Ziploc Bags for Low-Temp Cooking
*   **Material:** Polyethylene (food grade).
*   **Temperature Limits:**
    *   Safe for low-temperature cooking (up to ~60°C / 140°F).
    *   **Not Safe for Boiling:** Softens near boiling point (100°C / 212°F) and approaches melting point.
*   **Usage Context:**
    *   Suitable for sous vide (low temp) and cooking/chilling.
    *   **Not Suitable for Long-Term Preservation:** Lack vacuum seal integrity for storage.
    *   **Manufacturer Stance:** C. Johnson Wax (Ziploc maker) advises against cooking due to liability concerns regarding assumed usage beyond reheating, despite the material's technical suitability for low temps.

## Ingredients & Functional Properties

### Skirt Steak
*   **Characteristics:** Thin cut; prone to overcooking if seared directly from cold or cooked at low temps for short durations without searing.
*   **Cooking Strategies:**
    1.  **Short Duration (1–2 hours):** Requires higher temperature (~57°C / 135°F). Must sear immediately after cooking to prevent overcooking the thin cut.
    2.  **Long Duration (10+ hours):** Can cook at ribeye temperatures (~55°C / 130°F). Allows for a "double sear" (sear before and after) or a single hard sear after cooling.
    3.  **Pre-Searing:** Not recommended. Salt draws out moisture; pre-searing can lead to uneven curing or texture issues.
*   **Marinade:** Lime juice recommended for flavor. Do not salt until right before the final sear.

### Maple Syrup Grading
*   **Grade C:**
    *   **Characteristics:** Darker, more intense flavor, "funkier," earthier, mushroomy notes. Occurs later in the season.
    *   **Flavor Profile:** Can be rough, metallic in certain applications, and less compatible with delicate ingredients compared to Grade B.
    *   **Acquisition:** Often sold without labels; producers may mark it as "2 B's" to code for Grade C.
*   **Grade B:**
    *   **Characteristics:** Balanced flavor, less intense than C.
    *   **Usage:** Preferred for home cooking (pancakes) and cocktails.
*   **Grading Systems:** US system (A, B, C) differs from Canadian system.

### Cream Liqueur Emulsification
*   **Problem:** Phase separation (fat rising to top, watery liquid at bottom) in high-alcohol (22%) dairy mixtures.
*   **Causes:** Cold dairy, insufficient emulsification, lack of stabilizers.
*   **Solutions:**
    *   **Emulsifiers:** Gum Arabic (emulsifier) + Xanthan Gum (stabilizer).
    *   **Hardcore Emulsifiers:** Glycerol Monostearate (GSM) or Sodium Caseinate (if used incorrectly).
    *   **Process:** Dairy must be warm during initial mixing to facilitate emulsification.
    *   **Stability:** Separation can recur after sitting for 30+ minutes; requires robust stabilizers to prevent re-separation.

### Corn (Fresh vs. Frozen)
*   **Fresh:** Best flavor (July/August).
*   **Frozen Ear:** Inferior quality. Cooking frozen ears rarely matches fresh corn flavor.

### Soy Whipped Cream
*   **Base:** Soft tofu + Coconut milk.
*   **Stabilizers:** Required for siphon use.
*   **Result:** Works well out of the siphon.

## Techniques & Parameters

### Skirt Steak Low-Temp Protocol
*   **Option A (Short Cook):**
    *   Temp: 57°C (135°F).
    *   Time: ~1–2 hours.
    *   Post-Cook: Hard sear immediately.
*   **Option B (Long Cook):**
    *   Temp: 55°C (130°F).
    *   Time: 10+ hours.
    *   Post-Cook: Cool down, then hard sear.
*   **Marination:** Apply marinade (e.g., lime juice) before bagging. Do not salt until pre-sear (if double sear) or final sear.

### Pressure Cooked Jam/Jelly
*   **Theory:** Pressure cooking raises boiling point, potentially reducing volatile loss while cooking to required solids level.
*   **Feasibility:** Theoretically possible but difficult.
*   **Requirements:**
    *   Must know exact target solids level (typically 60–70%).
    *   Must calculate sugar/fruit ratios accurately to hit that solids level at the higher pressure temperature.
    *   Risk: Thermal breakdown of flavor despite reduced volatile loss.
    *   Method: Seal ingredients in a jar inside a pressure cooker (non-venting preferred for flavor retention).

### Double Sear Method
*   **Process:** Sear meat -> Low-temp cook -> Cool -> Sear again.
*   **Benefit:** Allows for a high-quality crust and perfectly tender interior.
*   **Risk:** Drying out the exterior if the second sear is not controlled carefully.
