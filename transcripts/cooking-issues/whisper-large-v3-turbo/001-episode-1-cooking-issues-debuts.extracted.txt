# Cooking Issues Podcast Notes

## Equipment

### Rotary Evaporator (Roto-Vap)
- **Manufacturer**: Büchi ("first name in rotary evaporation")
- **Cost**: $5,000–$18,000+
- **Function**: Low-temperature distillation equipment for separating flavors
- **Legal constraint**: Distillation of alcohol is illegal in the U.S. without a distiller's license; restaurants limited to non-alcoholic applications
- **Applications**:
  - Chocolate liquor (cocoa flavor without bitterness; bitterness doesn't distill)
  - Spicy peppers (habanero with floral aroma, no heat)
  - Fresh herbs (cilantro, etc.)
- **Advantage over commercial beverages**: Purer, cleaner flavors; difficult to commercialize due to short shelf life or production complexity

### Induction Cooktop
- **Brand mentioned**: CookTec
- **Advantage**: Most efficient cooking method; uses oscillating magnetic field to heat pan directly rather than heating air
- **Adoption barrier in U.S.**: Electricity more expensive than gas; chefs unfamiliar with technology
- **Popularity**: More common in Europe

### Vacuum Sealing Machine
- **Brand/Model**: Minipack Torre MVS31
- **Size**: Professional equipment suitable for home use
- **Applications**:
  - Sous vide cooking
  - Extending shelf life of leftovers (prevents oxidation)
  - Repackaging bulk meat from stores like Costco
  - Preserving blueberries without freezing/ice damage
  - Portioning cooked pasta for easy reheating

### Sous Vide Equipment

**Sous Vide Supreme**
- Cost: ~$500
- Type: Unstirred water bath
- Limitation: No circulation; larger footprint
- Best for: Longer-cooked items; less ideal for frequent bag changes or short-term cooking

**Immersion Circulator (PolyScience)**
- Cost: Just under $800
- Advantage: Stirred bath; compact (between stick blender and regular blender size); easier storage
- **Dave's assessment**: "I've never heard someone say they regretted buying a circulator"
- Recommendation: Prioritize circulator over Sous Vide Supreme for home use

## Techniques & Parameters

### French Fries (Advanced Method)

**Traditional double-fry approach**: Kindergarten-level technique

**Optimal multi-step process**:
1. **Enzyme soak**: Pectinex SPL (natural enzyme, breaks down pectin on fry exterior)
   - Duration: ~2 hours
   - Effect: Allows starch to swell quickly, forms superior crust; eliminates need for post-blanch drying
   - Source: Available at cookingissues.com

2. **Blanching**: Boiling salted water
   - Purpose: Kill enzymes, cook potatoes, flavor with salt
   - Note: Heston Blumenthal recommends blanching until fries break apart

3. **Frying**: Two fry cycles after blanching

**Goals**:
- Crispy, crunchy exterior (not greasy-tasting)
- Potato flavor and texture inside (not overcooked, not hollow)
- Maintains quality when cold (key differentiator from hot-only fries)

### Dry Ice for Chilling Drinks

**Properties**:
- Solid CO₂; easier to obtain/store than liquid nitrogen
- Higher cooling capacity than ice; lasts longer
- Cooling capacity similar to liquid nitrogen (though LN₂ is colder)
- Cost: ~$2/lb; typically sold in 20 lb minimum (~$40 total)
- Storage: Sublimates over time; cannot store long-term at home; lasts hours to days in cooler

**Advantages**:
- Maintains drink dilution (pre-batch at parties)
- Keeps drinks cold throughout event without refreezing
- Creates light carbonation (unavoidable side effect)
- Visual appeal (mist/fog)

**Best practices**:
- Use narrow pitcher (not wide sherbet-style bowl)
- Add 2–3 chunks; don't overload
- Stir after 10–15 minutes; drink becomes lightly carbonated and stays cold
- Add more chunks if supply runs low
- Works for traditional drinks (gin & tonics, daiquiris)

**Safety warnings**:
- **Never seal dry ice in bottles**: Pressure builds to 800+ psi; explosion risk
- **Wear gloves**: Cold burns from prolonged contact
- **Wear safety glasses**: Hard material; fragments can fly when chipping
- **Ventilation**: Don't use in sealed rooms (CO₂ buildup)
- **Don't serve chunks**: Risk of tongue burns

**Application example**: Gin & tonics
- Mix gin, tonic, lime in pitcher
- Add dry ice
- Stays carbonated and cold all evening
- Prevents flat, warm final sips

### Vacuum Sealing: Vacuum Level by Application

**High vacuum (98%+)**:
- **Use for**: Texture modification, long-term fridge/freezer storage
- **Purpose**: Remove all oxygen (prevents oxidation, warmed-over flavor in meat)
- **Requirement**: Product must be very cold before sealing

**Low vacuum**:
- **Use for**: Fish and poultry (especially if cooking sous vide)
- **Reason**: High vacuum causes textural degradation
  - Initial bite: extremely juicy
  - Subsequent chews: fibery, grainy texture (water rushes out)
  - Effect similar to pressing brick on meat during cooking
- **Recommendation**: Use minimum vacuum for good bag seal around food
- **Technique**: Bag fish with oil or liquid to prevent crushing

**Testing approach**: Cook three identical pieces at 98%, 99%, and full vacuum; taste differences

**Red meat**: Less sensitive to vacuum level than fish/poultry

### Sous Vide: Tenderloin

**Problem**: Becomes fibrous and mushy when cooked sous vide

**Root cause**: Tenderloin lacks connective tissue; overcooking effects happen quickly at low temps. Longer cooking = textural changes (fibery texture) even if not technically overcooked.

**Solution**:
- **Temperature**: 54°C (low end of scale; don't go higher)
- **Duration**: ~45 minutes only
- **Searing**: Essential (kills surface bacteria; inside muscle is sterile)
- **Food safety note**: 45 minutes doesn't meet food code (requires longer hold times), but searing makes it safe in practice. Thailand has no food code restrictions.

---

### Brisket

**Challenge**: Tough cut with high connective tissue; often comes out dry or undercooked

**Technique**: Low and slow braising
- **Temperature**: 250°F oven
- **Duration**: Long (depends on weight)
- **Key principle**: Overcook meat initially to break down collagen → collagen converts to gelatin → gelatin re-moistens meat
- **Common mistake**: Insufficient cooking time; collagen doesn't fully convert

**Searing**: Debated; Texas pit masters typically don't sear initially

**Flavor difference**: Braised brisket tastes like braise (delicious but different from smoked); commercial/pit-cooked brisket gets smoke ring and low-temperature confit-like treatment

**Note**: Dave promised forum discussion on cookingissues.com with input from Texas brisket experts

## Terminology & Philosophy

### "Molecular Gastronomy" (Critique)

**Dave's position**: Term is meaningless, misleading, and should be abandoned

**Problems**:
1. **Meaningless**: No clear definition; either all cooking manipulates molecules or none does
2. **Sounds disgusting**: "Molecular" + "gastronomy" (sounds like "gas") = unappealing branding
3. **Originated by Hervé This** (French scientist): Doesn't speak English well; thinks "food" sounds gross in English
4. **Pigeonholes chefs**: Chefs labeled "molecular gastronomy" don't want the label
5. **Creates false assumptions**: People assume new technologies = wacky, inedible food

**Reality**:
- New cooking technologies used widely in restaurants without fanfare
- Best applications are invisible to diner (e.g., crystal-clear strawberry drink with perfect effervescence)
- Foams, spheres, etc. are tools—not inherently good or bad
- **Key principle**: Techniques should improve flavor or achieve desired effect, not be used as gimmicks

**Comparison**: Handing someone a frying pan vs. centrifuge—both are tools; execution matters

## Ingredients & Functional Properties

### Pectinex SPL
- **Type**: Natural enzyme (not GMO)
- **Function**: Breaks down pectin on exterior of potatoes
- **Effect**: Allows starch to swell quickly, forms superior crust
- **Application**: French fries (soak ~2 hours before blanching)
- **Source**: cookingissues.com

- **Tanqueray**: High proof; easy to mix; Dave's instinctive reach for mixing

## Caller Q&A Summary

### Carolyn (Massachusetts): Dry Ice for Chilling Drinks
- **Q**: Benefits of chilling drinks with dry ice?
- **A**: Maintains dilution, keeps drinks cold all evening, creates light carbonation, visual appeal. Use 2–3 chunks in narrow pitcher; stir after 10–15 minutes. Works great for parties. Safety: wear gloves, glasses; never seal in bottles.

### John: Equipment Purchases & Vacuum Sealing
- **Q**: How much air to extract when vacuum sealing? Recommendations for vacuum level?
- **A**: Depends on application. High vacuum (98%+) for texture modification and long-term storage. Low vacuum for fish/poultry to avoid textural degradation (fibrousness). Test with identical pieces at different levels. Minipack MVS31 is excellent home equipment.

### John (continued): Sous Vide Equipment
- **Q**: Circulator vs. Sous Vide Supreme?
- **A**: Circulator superior: stirred bath, compact, easier storage, better for frequent bag changes. Sous Vide Supreme: unstirred, larger, ~$500 vs. circulator ~$800. Circulator worth extra $300. No regrets reported from circulator owners.

### Janelle: Brisket
- **Q**: Brisket always comes out terrible. Should I sear first? Low temp in oven?
- **A**: Brisket is tough cut; needs low-and-slow braising to break down collagen into gelatin (re-moistens meat). 250°F oven for long duration. Common mistake: insufficient cooking time. Searing debated; Texas pit masters typically don't. Braised brisket tastes like braise (delicious but different from smoked). Forum discussion promised on cookingissues.com.

### WeePop Soupy Pot (Bangkok, Thailand): Sous Vide Tenderloin
- **Q**: Why does tenderloin become fibrous and mushy sous vide?
- **A**: Tenderloin lacks connective tissue; textural changes happen quickly at low temps. Cook at 54°C for ~45 minutes only. Sear to kill surface bacteria (inside is sterile). Don't overcook. 45 min doesn't meet U.S. food code but is safe with searing; Thailand has no code restrictions.
