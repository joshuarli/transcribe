# Cooking Issues Podcast Notes

## Rotary Evaporator (Rotovap)

**Equipment & Legal Status**
- Expensive equipment ($5,000–$18,000+)
- Used for distillation at low temperatures
- **Illegal for alcohol distillation in US without distiller's license**
- Restaurants in US limited to non-alcoholic distillations (not compelling enough to justify cost/space)
- A few restaurants in Chicago use them legally
- Buki is primary manufacturer ("first name in rotary evaporation")

**Applications & Flavor Extraction**
- Separates flavors impossible to achieve other ways
- **Chocolate liquor**: cocoa flavor without bitterness (bitterness doesn't distill, aroma does)
- **Habanero peppers**: floral aroma without spice
- **Fresh herbs**: cilantro and others—cleaner than any other method
- Flavors often difficult to commercialize: don't last long, expensive to produce, or hard to scale
- Non-commercial applications yield purest, cleanest flavors possible

## French Fries

**Problem Statement**
- Must be crispy outside, not greasy-tasting
- Potato flavor and texture must be good inside (not overcooked, undercooked, or hollow)
- Must stay good when cold (key differentiator)
- "Any monkey can make a french fry good when hot"

**Traditional Double-Fry Technique**
- First fry: form crust and cook
- Second fry: make crispy again
- Described as "kindergarten French fry"—not optimal

**Optimized Multi-Step Process**
1. **Enzyme soak**: Use Pectinex SPL (natural enzyme, not GMO)
   - Breaks down pectin on outside of fries
   - Allows starch on outside to swell quickly
   - Forms excellent crust
   - Eliminates need for post-blanch drying
   - Soak duration: couple of hours

2. **Blanch**: Boiling salted water
   - Cooks potatoes
   - Flavors potatoes (salt penetration)
   - Kills enzymes
   - No drying step needed after blanch

3. **Fry twice** after blanching

**Result**: "Arguably the most delicious French fries"

**Resources**: Details at www.cookingissues.com; Pectinex SPL available from site

## Dry Ice for Drink Chilling

**Caller: Carolyn (Massachusetts)**

**Properties**
- Solid CO₂
- Easier to obtain and store than liquid nitrogen
- More cooling capacity than ice; similar to liquid nitrogen
- Cannot carry over bridges easily
- Cost: ~$2/lb, minimum purchase ~20 lbs (~$40 investment)
- Available from ice supply houses or welding suppliers

**Advantages**
- Chill pre-batched drinks to exact dilution before service
- Keep drinks cold for hours without diluting them (unlike ice)
- Stays cold throughout party/event
- Creates visual appeal (mist effect)

**Disadvantages & Precautions**
- Small chips of dry ice can burn mouth/tongue if ingested
- **Never seal dry ice in bottles/containers**: reaches ~800 psi or higher, causing explosion
- Risk of cold burns from handling—**use gloves**
- **Wear safety glasses** when chipping (pieces are hard, can fly)
- **Do not use in sealed living spaces**: CO₂ buildup dangerous
- Will cause light carbonation in drinks (unavoidable)

**Technique for Parties**
- Use narrow pitcher (not wide sherbet bowl style)
- Add 1–2 chunks of dry ice at bottom
- Stir occasionally
- After 10–15 minutes: drink is lightly carbonated and stays cold all night
- Can add more dry ice if needed
- Storage: In styrofoam cooler; lasts hours to 1+ day depending on insulation
- Cannot store long-term at home (sublimes away)

**Application: Gin & Tonics**
- Traditional drinks often turn flat, warm, nasty
- Pre-batch in pitcher with dry ice: stays carbonated and cold
- Allows extended outdoor serving without quality degradation

## Vacuum Sealing/Vacuum Packing

**Equipment**: Minipack Torre MVS31 (professional home-sized machine)

**Applications**
- Sous vide cooking
- Extending shelf life of leftovers (prevents oxidation)
- Repackaging bulk meat (e.g., Costco) into usable portions
- Preserving berries (low vac): keeps without freezing-related issues
- Portioning cooked pasta for easy reheating

**Vacuum Levels: General Rules**

**For texture modification or freezer storage**
- Use heaviest vacuum possible (98–100%)
- Removes all oxygen to prevent warmed-over flavor
- **Requirement**: Product must be very cold before vacuuming (otherwise poor seal)

**For Fish** (critical)
- Use **minimum vacuum** (low vac), just enough for bag seal
- High vacuum causes texture problems when cooked:
  - Initial bite extremely juicy
  - Water rushes out during chewing
  - Becomes fibery, grainy texture by 6th–7th chew
  - Similar to crushing fish under a brick
- **Recommendation**: Bag with oil or liquid to prevent crushing

**For Poultry**
- Use **low vacuum** if vacuum-packing
- Preferred method: seal in Ziplocs without vacuum

**Testing Protocol** (for home cooks)
- If time permits, cook three identical portions at 98%, 99%, and full vacuum
- Cook identically and taste differences

**Commercial Equipment vs. Consumer**
- FoodSaver: guided consumer adoption but not ideal
- Professional machines vastly superior
- MiniPak working on price reduction

**Upcoming Resource**: Vacuum packing section planned for www.cookingissues.com low-temp sous vide primer

## Sous Vide Equipment

**Immersion Circulators**
- New PolyScience circulator coming to market (under $800)
- Size: between stick blender and regular blender
- Advantages over unstirred baths:
  - Stirred bath ensures even heating
  - Better for short-term items or frequent additions/removals
  - Easier to store than Sous Vide Supreme
  - $300 more than Sous Vide Supreme (~$800 vs ~$500) well-spent per Dave

**Strong Endorsement**: "Never heard someone say they regretted buying a circulator"

**Sous Vide Supreme**
- Unstirred bath
- Holds product well
- ~$500
- Not bad, but larger footprint
- Less ideal for short-term items or frequent access
- Can accommodate only one bag at a time efficiently

**Multi-Temperature Cooking**
- Can do vegetables and meat at different temps if multiple devices available
- Circulator allows staggered cooking with separate baths

## Brisket Cooking

**Caller: Janelle**

**Problems Encountered**
- Grilling ineffective (cannot maintain low temps)
- Oven method (~250°F, long duration) inconsistent
- Unsure about searing or other technique gaps

**Fundamental Approach**
- Brisket = tough cut with lots of connective tissue
- Cook at low temperature for extended time
- Overcook the meat intentionally to break down collagen
- Collagen converts to gelatin → re-moistens meat
- If inadequately cooked: meat stays dry

**Cooking Method: Braise-Style**
- Low and slow temperature essential
- Must allow sufficient time for collagen conversion
- Not traditional "smoke ring" barbecue (commercial pit method)

**Commercial Technique**
- Extremely low heat pit smoking
- Smoke gives characteristic smoke ring
- Essentially confit-like cooking (low temp, extended time) without added fat
- Stays moist due to collagen breakdown + rendered collagen gelatin

**Distinction**
- Braising method will produce delicious meat but won't taste like traditional brisket (tastes "braised")
- Smoke/low-temp pit produces characteristic brisket flavor

**Resource**: Forum discussion to be posted at www.cookingissues.com forums within one week with detailed technique; Texas barbecue experts expected to contribute

**Marination Note**: 24-hour marination before cooking improves retention of moisture/flavor

## Tenderloin (Sous Vide)

**Caller: WeePop Soupy Pot (Bangkok, Thailand)**

**Problem**: Tenderloin becomes fibrous and mushy when sous vide cooked

**Root Cause**
- Tenderloin has minimal connective tissue
- Requires extremely low temperatures
- Temperature + time interaction differs from high-collagen cuts
- Extended cooking at low temp causes textural degradation (fibery texture) even if not technically overcooked
- Low-connective-tissue meat degrades texture with prolonged exposure, unlike collagen-rich cuts that improve

**Optimal Parameters**
- Temperature: ~54°C (lower end of scale)
- Duration: ~45 minutes maximum
- Must sear before cooking (kills surface bacteria)
- Minimize cooking time

**Food Safety Note**
- 45 minutes insufficient for food code compliance (no time-temperature safety margin)
- However, safer in practice: searing kills exterior bacteria; muscle interior is sterile
- Not code-compliant in US, but effective
- Thailand has no code restrictions

**Searing Benefit**: Kills all exterior bacteria, making short sous vide cook safer in practice

## "Molecular Gastronomy" Terminology Critique

**Dave's Position**: Term is meaningless, harmful, and should be abandoned

**Reasons**
1. **Meaningless**: No clear definition
2. **Poor branding**:
   - "Molecular" sounds disgusting, not appetizing
   - "Gastronomy" sounds like gas/gastropod; implies you'll get gas
   - Compound term sounds inherently off-putting
3. **Origin**: Coined by Hervé This (French scientist), who doesn't speak English well and thinks "food" sounds gross
4. **Misattribution**: None of the chefs labeled with term accept it; creates unwanted pigeonhole
5. **Logical inconsistency**: Either all cooking manipulates molecules (including campfire cooking), or none do; no one uses scanning tunneling microscopes to adjust individual molecules
6. **Marketing construct**: Purely commercial term with no scientific basis

**Real Issue**
- New cooking technologies used widely in restaurants without notice
- People assume technology = wacky/unusual food
- Best applications invisible: consumers experience delicious food without knowing equipment used
- Example: crystal-clear strawberry drink with perfect effervescence and fresh liquor flavor—consumer thinks "delicious drink," not "this required $30k equipment and 5 hours"

**Best Practice**: Use techniques only when they improve food, not as gimmicks
- Tools are neutral (frying pan or centrifuge can make good or bad food)
- Technique without purpose = poor application
- Occasional silly gimmicks acceptable but not the goal

**Foams Misunderstanding**
- Foams not new (beer head is ancient foam)
- Problem: people make foam just to make foam, not for flavor benefit
- When combined with poor food = entire technique gets blamed

## Gin & Tonic Discussion

**Caller: John (preferred CookTec induction and Minipack vacuum sealer)**

**Dave's Gin Preferences**
- Hendrix: favorite for some applications (pricey)
- Tanqueray: hand instinctively reaches for; high proof, mixes well
- Bombay (white label, not Sapphire): father's preference; source of Dave's love of gin/tonic

**Gateway Gin**: Plymouth
- Light on botanical flavors (especially juniper)
- Juniper often objectionable to gin newcomers
- Good entry point

**Other Options**
- Oxley: fine, different taste profile (not traditional)
- Beefeater: popular among bartenders

**Recommendation**: Taste multiple gins; personal preference varies widely

**Gin & Tonic Service Issue** (general)
- Usually either under-carbonated or not boozy enough
- Meant as summertime drink
- Often executed poorly (flat, warm final sips)
- **Solution**: Use dry ice in pitcher to maintain carbonation + temperature throughout service

## Equipment: Induction Cooktop

**Model Mentioned**: CookTec induction unit

**Properties**
- Uses oscillating electromagnetic field to heat pan (not conductive heat)
- Most electrically efficient cooking method
- Doesn't heat kitchen air
- Not as popular in US (gas cheaper than electricity; chefs unfamiliar)
- More prevalent in Europe

## Culinary Philosophy

**Technique vs. Gimmick**
- Technique marriage with flavor essential
- Never use technique just to use it
- Tools are neutral; application determines quality

**Commercial Distillates vs. Homemade**
- Commercial beverages delicious but different flavors achievable
- Roto-vap distillates: purest, cleanest flavors
- Challenge: commercial viability (short shelf life, difficulty scaling, expense)
