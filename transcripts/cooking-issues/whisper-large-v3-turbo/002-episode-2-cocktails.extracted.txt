## Curing & Preservation

### Vacuum-Sealed Curing
- **Pastrami with beef blade**: Vacuum bagging accelerates cure penetration significantly compared to traditional brining
- **Advantages of vacuum curing**: Uses less brine, allows drier rubs, cures quickly
- **Curing agents**: Instacure powder or Morton's Tender Quick (salt + nitrates); nitrites have been used for centuries/millennia in salt deposits naturally
- **Penetration speed**: Pork belly can cure almost completely overnight in vacuum bag
- **Caveat for long-cured items**: Don't accelerate cures on items like ham where extended curing time develops flavor; vacuum acceleration not recommended for these

## Sous Vide / Low-Temperature Cooking

### Chicken Breast (Skinless)
- **Temperature**: 63°C for ~1 hour; can increase to 64°C if firmer texture desired; do not exceed 65°C
- **Bagging method**: Use Ziploc bags rather than vacuum seal to avoid "canned chicken" texture (too juicy, juices flow out on bite, subsequent chews dry)
- **Ziploc technique**: Seal bag almost completely, then dunk under water to displace air for natural seal
- **Liquid**: Can use chicken stock or olive oil; avoid highly flavorful oils (taste interaction can be strange)
- **Brining**: Not necessary for preventing overcooking (temperature control prevents that), but increases salt level inside meat for better taste/texture
- **Best use**: Chopped for salads or cold preparations; texture remains delicious when cooled

### General Sous Vide Principles
- **Immersion circulator**: Equipment that maintains precise water temperature; prevents overcooking, no monitoring needed
- **Penetration time calculation**: 
  - Use Douglas Baldwin's sous vide primer or Nathan Mirvold's tables (eGOULET website) for accurate timing
  - Avoid "X minutes per pound" formulas—heating is not linear with size
  - Examples: egg ~45 min–1 hour; rolled chicken ~1 hour; 36-inch striped bass ~3 hours
- **Thermometer use**: Fraught with problems—difficult to place correctly, can puncture bag causing leaks; often causes more problems than help

## Large Lobster Cooking

### 20-Pound Lobster Technique
- **Problem with large lobsters**: Cooked too long (becomes mushy) or served improperly; muscle fibers become coarser with age
- **Solution**: 
  1. Steam 8 minutes (kills lobster, separates meat from shell)
  2. Use metal shears to cut meat from thick shell
  3. Slice meat into thin discs (shortens fibers, allows chewing with grain)
  4. Ziploc bag slices with butter
  5. Quick-poach individual slices to preserve texture
- **Result**: Different texture from young lobster but still delicious; prevents toughness and mushiness

## Cocktails

### Pre-Batching Stirred Cocktails
- **Shaken vs. stirred**: Blind taste tests show clear difference; shaking adds texture, stirring does not
- **Pre-batching limitation**: Hard to pre-batch shaken cocktails (need shaking for texture); stirred cocktails pre-batch well if diluted properly and chilled
- **Liquid nitrogen option**: Can emulate shaken texture in pre-batched drinks using liquid nitrogen chilling
- **Determining water content for pre-batch**:
  1. Make single drink by volume (using jiggers)
  2. Weigh drink on accurate scale
  3. Add ice, stir normally, strain
  4. Weigh final cocktail
  5. Subtract weight of liquor/ingredients from final weight = water to add
  6. Accuracy within 2–3 grams
  7. Write recipe down; reusable for future batches
- **Note**: Don't dilute at room temperature and taste—balance changes when chilled

### Cocktail Science (Tales of the Cocktail seminar)
- **Shaking variables**: Ice type and shaking method make minimal difference to temperature/dilution (within reasonable limits); affects texture in mouth more than anything

## Pâte de Fruit (Fruit Paste/Jelly)

### Recipe Overview (Michael Ascanis)
1. Cook fruit puree to 180°F with sugar
2. Add pectin, sugar, glucose syrup
3. Bring to 225°F
4. Pour onto silpat to set

### Burning/Browning Problem
- **Issue**: Fruit browning/burning at 180°F, worsens with added sugar
- **Likely cause**: Pan temperature too hot, causing scorching
- **Solutions**:
  - Use induction burner for accurate temperature control (prevents scorching better than open flame)
  - Cook slowly
  - 225°F should be achievable without burning with good pan temperature control
- **Water bath alternative**: Cannot reach 225°F in water bath without pressure; water bath doesn't remove water needed for proper set
- **Rotovap possibility** (untested): Could potentially do low-temperature pâte de fruit by dissolving pectin at boiling, then evaporating water under vacuum to reach target solids content

### Pectin Function
- **Requirements**: Needs heat (boiling or near), acidity, high soluble solids (sugar)
- **Why it works**: Sugar binds remaining water so pectin gels properly
- **Common failure**: Insufficient acid or insufficient cooking time prevents setting
- **Alternative**: Low-methoxyl amidated (LMA) pectin allows lower soluble solids without high-temperature cooking (not widely available)

## Mana (Dried Tree Sap/Honeydew Candy)

### Definition & Formation
- **Criteria**: Sweet, appears from nowhere (wild/uncultivated), providential
- **Sources**:
  - Dried sap from trees (bugs chew undersides of leaves, sap dries)
  - Honeydew: bug bites leaf/stem, sap pressure forces through bug's body, exits as dried sweet substance
- **Geography**: Primarily Middle East (dry climate allows formation)
- **Harvesting**: People beat bushes with twigs in morning, collect falling mana

### Mana Candy (Gaz)
- **Composition**: Mana + sugar + nuts (possibly egg whites)
- **Texture**: Chewy but breaks; unique mana-like quality
- **Taste variation**: Each bite tastes different; different people perceive differently
- **Availability**: Saffron King (website) sells straight mana mixed in bourbon
